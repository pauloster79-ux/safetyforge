## 1. THE WORLD WE'RE BUILDING TOWARD

It is 2029. The construction industry has changed — not in the way the trade magazines predicted, with drones and robots and 3D-printed houses, but in the way that actually matters: the contractor who used to juggle six apps, three binders, and a filing cabinet now runs everything through one tool. Safety, daily logs, time tracking, quality inspections, estimating, job costing, sub compliance, scheduling — all of it, through a single conversational interface that speaks the language of the jobsite.

The change did not come from enterprise software trickling down to small contractors. Procore still costs $30,000 a year, and the 50-person GC in Texas still cannot justify it. The change came from a platform that started with the hardest, most regulated part of construction operations — safety compliance — and expanded outward, using a knowledge graph that connects every worker, every certification, every project, every regulation, every cost line, and every daily log entry into one intelligent system. The contractor talks to Kerf — by chat or by voice — and Kerf understands, acts, and remembers.

Marco Gutierrez runs a concrete crew of 22 in Phoenix. Three years ago, his morning looked like this: check the weather on his phone, open one app for the safety checklist, open another for the daily log, open a third for time tracking, open email for RFI responses, check a spreadsheet for the schedule, and spend Sunday night reconciling it all. His safety program was a binder on a shelf. His daily logs were fiction written from memory. His timesheets were paper. His punch lists were sticky notes. He had no idea if his jobs were making money until they were finished.

Today, Marco opens Kerf at 5:30 AM. The morning brief is waiting — risk score, weather, expiring certifications, yesterday's open corrective actions, today's schedule, job cost status across his three active projects, and a toolbox talk ready in English and Spanish. He delivers the talk to his crew (they sign off on their phones). He walks the site with his phone recording — the AI extracts the safety inspection, quality observations, crew count, equipment status, and weather conditions from his narration and photos. His daily log is 80% pre-filled from data the system already captured. He reviews it, adds the work-completed narrative, and taps confirm. His crew clocked in via GPS when they arrived. Kerf flagged that two workers' fall protection certifications expire Wednesday, so they need renewal before Friday's pour. And it noticed that the daily log mentioned relocating formwork that was not in the original scope — it drafted a change order with photos and time entries attached, waiting for Marco to review.

For Marco, the change is not that he has better software. The change is that he has one tool instead of six, and that tool does the thinking. His EMR is 0.78. His insurance premiums dropped $52,000 last year. He won three bids he would have been disqualified from in 2026. His crew has gone 847 days without a lost-time incident. He captured $34,000 in change orders last year that he would have previously eaten. And he spends less time on paperwork now than he did when he was faking it.

For Sarah Chen, who owns a 45-person electrical contracting firm in Atlanta, the change means she got her Sundays back — and her evenings too. She used to spend 3-5 hours every evening estimating jobs at the kitchen table, and four hours every weekend reconciling everything else. Now she estimates a job by walking the site and talking to Kerf — 20 minutes during the walkthrough, and the proposal goes to the GC by lunch. Job costing happens automatically from time entries and daily logs. Change orders are flagged and drafted before she even knows about them. When she asks "how did we do on the Peachtree job?", Kerf tells her not just the margin, but why — which crew was fast, where the delays were, which change orders she captured and which she missed.

For Jake Torres, a solo electrician who went out on his own two years ago, the change is that he finally has a business, not just a job. He used to estimate in his head, track his hours on paper, and find out whether he made money by checking his bank balance. Now he talks to Kerf while standing in the client's kitchen — "200-amp panel upgrade, 12 new circuits, 6 receptacles in the island" — and has a structured estimate in 30 seconds. After 20 jobs, Kerf knows what his panel upgrades actually cost and what his real labour rates are. He bids with confidence instead of guessing. He catches every extra the homeowner asks for. He has a safety program for the first time — and it took him zero effort.

For the industry, the change is structural. Insurance carriers have granular real-time data from small contractors — not annual self-reported summaries, but daily inspection results, time-on-site records, quality deficiency rates, incident reports, cost performance, and compliance scores. General contractors can see every subcontractor's real-time compliance, training currency, and performance metrics. And construction workers around the world — the Spanish-speaking crew in Phoenix, the Polish crew in London, the Hindi-speaking crew in Dubai — receive safety information, daily instructions, and operational tools in their language, at their literacy level, every single day.

This is the world Kerf exists to build. Not a world where construction paperwork is easier to fill out, but a world where documentation is a natural byproduct of doing the work. Not a world with a better safety app, a better daily log app, a better time tracker, and a better estimating tool, but a world where the contractor opens one app, talks to it, and everything they need is there — informed by every conversation they have ever had with it, every document they have ever uploaded, and every job they have ever run.

Eleven hundred people died on construction sites in the United States last year. Most of them worked for companies with fewer than 50 employees. Most of those companies had no safety director, incomplete daily logs, paper timesheets, and no affordable way to change any of it. We are building the thing that changes that.

## 2. OUR BELIEFS

**We believe safety is the foundation, not a silo.** Safety touches everything on a construction site — scheduling, workforce, equipment, quality, compliance, costs. A platform that starts with safety and expands outward has a natural architecture, because the safety data enriches every other feature. An inspection engine that does safety also does quality. A worker record that tracks certifications also tracks time. A daily log that captures safety observations also captures production progress. Safety is the connective tissue of construction operations, and we build from that centre.

**We believe documentation should be a byproduct of doing the work, not a separate activity.** If a contractor has to stop building to start documenting, we have failed. The inspection log should come from the inspection, not from a memory of the inspection four days later. The daily log should come from the daily work, not from Sunday-night catch-up sessions. The toolbox talk record should come from the toolbox talk, not from someone scribbling names on a sign-in sheet after the crew has already walked away.

**We believe every worker deserves information in their language, regardless of their employer's budget or their country.** A labourer who speaks Spanish in Phoenix, Polish in London, Hindi in Dubai, or Vietnamese in Sydney should not receive worse safety communication, weaker training, or less access to operational tools than an English-speaking colleague. Multilingual support is not a premium feature, a regional add-on, or a Phase 2 addition — it is a fundamental obligation that scales with every market we enter.

**We believe the person running the work is the most important user in construction technology, and the most ignored.** Whether that is a solo electrician, the owner of a 5-person pool company, or the superintendent of a 100-person mechanical contractor — the person who actually manages the work, the crew, and the risk is an afterthought in every software product built for someone sitting at a desk. We build for them first. If it does not work in the field, it does not work.

**We believe small contractors are not less safe because they care less — they are less safe because they have less help.** The owner of a 25-person roofing company is not cutting corners out of malice. She is cutting corners because she is also the estimator, the project manager, the HR department, and the accounts receivable clerk. She has fifteen minutes a week for safety, and the compliance system demands forty hours. We close that gap.

**We believe AI in construction must be auditable, cited, and humble.** Every recommendation Kerf makes must trace back to a specific standard, regulation, or data point. We never present AI judgment as authoritative truth. We show our work. We tell users when we are uncertain. We make it easy to verify. The cost of a wrong answer in this domain is not a bad search result — it is a person going home in an ambulance.

**We believe compliance should be a competitive advantage, not just a cost of doing business.** Better compliance leads to lower EMR, lower premiums, more bid eligibility, and more revenue. Safety should make contractors money, and we prove it.

**We believe the phone in the contractor's pocket is the most powerful tool on the jobsite.** More powerful than any sensor, wearable, or IoT device. It has a camera, a microphone, GPS, internet access, and it is already there. We do not need new hardware. We need software that uses the hardware construction workers already carry.

**We believe one tool is better than six.** Construction workers average 6.2 separate technology products. Each one adds cognitive load, data silos, and adoption friction. The contractor who does their safety walk, daily log, time check, quality inspection, estimate, and schedule review in one app will never go back to five. Consolidation is not a feature — it is the product.

**We believe the estimate, the task list, and the cost report are the same data.** An estimate line — "84 standard receptacles, 29.4 hours, $294 material" — is simultaneously a task to assign to a crew and a cost line to track against. When software treats these as three separate modules requiring three rounds of data entry, it has already failed the contractor. In Kerf, one data structure flows through its lifecycle: estimated, then scheduled, then tracked, then analysed. No re-entry. No reconciliation. No Sunday night.

**We believe financial intelligence should be derived, not entered.** A 15-person electrical sub will never set up cost code structures, code every expense, enter every purchase order, and reconcile budgets monthly. The vast majority track finances by gut feel and find out whether they made money after the job is done. Kerf derives financial insight from data the contractor already captures in daily operations — time entries become labour costs, daily logs reveal delay causes, photos become change order evidence. The contractor never enters financial data. The system assembles it from the graph.

**We believe the knowledge graph is the moat, not the AI.** Every competitor will add AI. LLMs will get cheaper, faster, and more capable every year. What cannot be replicated quickly is a structured knowledge graph that connects every worker to every certification to every project to every regulation to every cost line to every daily log entry. The graph is what makes AI answers deterministic, auditable, and citable. Without it, AI is guessing. With it, AI is traversing. That structural difference is the defensible advantage.

**We believe the knowledge graph is not a backend — it is a universal substrate.** Competitors ship features as modules. Each module has its own schema, its own UI, its own reports, its own release cycle. When they want a new feature, they build a new module. Kerf ships capabilities as traversals of one connected graph. A new feature is mostly prompt engineering and ontology extension, not new code plus new schema plus new UI. This is how a small team ships what a larger competitor team cannot match — not because we are smarter, but because their schema was wrong. The graph is why every Kerf feature is richer than its relational equivalent, and why new capabilities are dramatically cheaper to add as the product matures. Customers do not buy "the graph." They buy what it makes possible. But internally, the graph is what Kerf is.

**We believe every conversation is data.** When a contractor says "I'm using 0.38 hours per receptacle because our guys are slower in renovations with low ceilings," that reasoning is as valuable as the number itself. Conversations — chat and voice — contain decisions, institutional knowledge, supplier relationships, GC preferences, and hard-won experience that no structured form could capture. Every interaction with Kerf is recorded, linked to the entities it touches, and available for the agent to draw on in future interactions. The contractor's knowledge stops walking out the door.

**We believe every document the contractor uploads makes the system smarter.** Plans, specs, contracts, COIs, permits, supplier quotes — these are not just files to store. They are knowledge to extract, structure, and connect. When plans are uploaded, the agent can cross-reference fixture counts against the estimate. When a contract is uploaded, the system knows the scope, the value, and the payment terms. When a COI is uploaded, it is parsed and monitored for expiry. Documents are not attachments — they are intelligence.

## 3. WHAT KERF DOES

Kerf supports every process a contractor runs, from finding work through getting paid. The data structures are scale-invariant — a solo electrician and a 100-person sub run the same processes; only the complexity and concurrency scale up. Every process is accessed conversationally (chat or voice), every interaction is recorded in the knowledge graph, and the agent acts within boundaries the contractor controls.

### 3.1 Find and Qualify Work

A lead arrives — a phone call, an email, a GC invitation to bid. The contractor tells Kerf about it. The system creates a Project in "lead" status, linked to the client Contact and any documents received. The agent assesses: is this the right kind of work? Do we have the right certifications? What is our capacity? How has this GC paid us before?

**What the contractor does:** "Peterson GC wants us to bid the electrical on a medical office on 4th Street." **What the agent does:** Creates the project, links the GC contact, checks crew availability, surfaces past job history with this GC, flags any certification gaps for the work type.

### 3.2 Estimate and Price

The contractor walks the site or reviews plans and talks through what they see. The agent captures WorkItems — discrete pieces of deliverable work, each with labour and material requirements. WorkItems can be grouped into WorkPackages for larger projects, or listed flat for simple jobs. The contractor never needs to know the difference — the structure adapts to the scale.

Each WorkItem carries its own cost estimate: labour hours multiplied by rate, materials multiplied by unit cost, plus equipment if needed. Costs are bottom-up — the project total is the sum of its work items plus margin. On day one, the contractor enters quantities and prices as they always have. After a few jobs, the graph shows patterns from their own history. After many jobs, the agent drafts estimates from historical data — actual crew productivity, real material costs, similar past projects.

When the contractor uploads plans, specs, or other project documents, the system reads them — extracting fixture counts, dimensions, material requirements, and specification clauses. The agent cross-references plan quantities against the estimate and flags requirements not yet covered.

**What the contractor does:** Walks the site, talks through what they see. Uploads the plans. Reviews and adjusts. **What the agent does:** Captures structured WorkItems from conversation. Extracts quantities from uploaded plans. Applies historical labour rates and current supplier pricing. Flags regulatory requirements for this jurisdiction and work type. Calculates margin and total.

### 3.3 Propose and Win

The contractor sends a proposal to the client or GC, referencing a versioned Estimate — a snapshot of WorkItems at agreed prices on a specific date. The client accepts, declines, or negotiates. When accepted, the Estimate becomes the basis for a Contract with agreed terms: value, retention percentage, payment schedule, key dates.

The project status moves from "lead" through "estimating" to "proposed" to "awarded." A lead IS a Project — no separate entity, no data re-entry. The estimate IS the scope — no separate scope document that diverges from what was priced.

**What the contractor does:** "Send the proposal to Peterson GC." Reviews the contract terms. **What the agent does:** Generates the proposal document. Tracks proposal status. Parses the signed contract for key terms (retention, payment schedule, milestones). Links everything to the project graph.

### 3.4 Plan and Mobilise

The awarded project needs a plan: who is working, when, with what materials and equipment. WorkItems from the estimate become the schedule — they already have labour, materials, and assignments. The contractor adds planned start and end dates, assigns workers or crews, and sets dependencies (rough-in before drywall).

Scheduling is a weekly lookahead — a rolling 2-4 week view, not a Gantt chart. Connected to everything: worker certifications (does this worker's cert expire before the scheduled task?), equipment availability (is the excavator free?), weather (rain forecast for the day of the pour?), trade sequencing (electrical before drywall), and cost impact.

The agent checks for conflicts automatically. The contractor says "move the pour to Friday" and the agent checks everything downstream — cert validity, equipment, weather, trade dependencies — and flags issues before they become problems.

**What the contractor does:** Assigns crews and dates. Adjusts the lookahead as conditions change. **What the agent does:** Generates the initial schedule from WorkItems. Detects conflicts with certifications, equipment, weather, and dependencies. Orders materials for upcoming work. Sends notifications to workers and subs.

### 3.5 Execute and Document

This is where the contractor spends 90% of their time. Every day on every active project:

**Daily operations.** Workers clock in (one tap, GPS-verified). The crew lead delivers the toolbox talk (in the workforce's languages). The crew lead walks the site — narrating and photographing — and the agent extracts the safety inspection, quality observations, crew count, equipment status, and weather conditions. The daily log is 80% pre-filled from data already captured. The crew lead adds the work-completed narrative and taps confirm. One site walk, three reports, no forms.

**Safety.** Inspections, hazard observations, incident reporting — all voice-first. The agent classifies hazards, generates reports, assigns corrective actions, tracks them to closure, and crosses-references with regulatory requirements for the jurisdiction. Workers report hazards in their own language with a photo and a voice note.

**Quality.** The same inspection engine as safety, with different templates referencing building codes instead of safety regulations. One walk produces both safety and quality reports. Deficiency items are tracked through identification, assignment, correction, and verification.

**Time tracking.** GPS-verified clock-in/clock-out linked to the project and the specific WorkItem being performed. Time entries automatically become cost data — labour hours multiplied by rate, compared to the estimate. The agent flags workers approaching overtime, fatigue thresholds, or certification expiry.

**Progress tracking.** As WorkItems are completed, the project's progress updates automatically. The contractor sees — at any moment — what work remains, what percentage is complete, and whether they are on track against the estimate.

**What the contractor does:** The work. Talks to Kerf during their normal routine. **What the agent does:** Captures structured data from every conversation, photo, and time entry. Auto-populates the daily log. Runs safety checks. Tracks quality deficiencies. Monitors progress against estimate and schedule. Flags issues proactively.

### 3.6 Manage Money

Job costing is auto-derived — never entered manually. Time entries flow to labour cost. Material deliveries flow to material cost. The agent connects these to the original estimate and surfaces the picture: "You are 4 days into the electrical rough-in, tracking 18% over on labour. The daily logs show the crew waited 6 hours last week for the framing crew. If you submit the delay documentation as a variation, you are actually under budget."

Variations (scope changes — known as change orders in the US) are detected automatically. When the daily log mentions work outside the original scope, the agent recognises it, assembles the evidence chain — daily log entry, photos, original versus revised plans, time entries, material costs — and drafts a variation. Contractors capture revenue they currently miss because nobody wrote it up.

**What the contractor does:** Asks "how are we doing on this job?" **What the agent does:** Traverses the graph: time entries versus estimated hours, material deliveries versus estimated materials, approved variations, delays documented in daily logs. Returns a narrative answer with the reasoning, not just numbers. Proactively flags when costs are trending over estimate and recommends actions.

### 3.7 Close Out

As the project nears completion: the deficiency list is generated from quality inspections, assigned, tracked to closure, and verified. The contractor walks the site for final inspection. As-built documentation is assembled from daily logs, photos, and inspection records — the system already has most of it.

Warranty obligations are recorded with scope, dates, and terms. The agent monitors warranty periods and surfaces issues if the client reports a problem during the warranty window.

**What the contractor does:** Final walkthrough. Reviews deficiency list. Confirms completion. **What the agent does:** Generates the deficiency list from accumulated quality observations. Tracks each item to verified closure. Assembles closeout documentation. Records warranty terms.

### 3.8 Get Paid

The contractor invoices the client — either on completion (simple jobs) or as progress billing (commercial work with staged payments). Invoice lines reference specific WorkItems and their completion percentage. Approved variations are included. The agent generates the invoice, syncs to QuickBooks, and tracks payment status.

Payment applications (formal progress claims common on commercial work) reference the contract value, work completed to date, retention held, and previous payments. The agent assembles these from data already in the graph.

Kerf never replaces the accounting system. QuickBooks (or Xero, or Sage) handles general ledger, tax, and payroll. Kerf handles the intelligence — the connected understanding of what work was done, what it cost, and what is owed. Financial data syncs between the two.

**What the contractor does:** "Bill Peterson GC for this month's progress." **What the agent does:** Calculates percent complete from WorkItem progress. Includes approved variations not yet billed. Generates the invoice or payment application. Syncs to the accounting system. Monitors payment against terms. Alerts if payment is late.

### 3.9 Learn

After the job closes, the contractor needs to know: did we make money, and why? The agent traverses the full graph — estimated versus actual costs by WorkItem, crew productivity, delays, variations captured versus missed, material waste, schedule adherence — and produces a narrative post-mortem.

This intelligence feeds forward. The next time the contractor estimates similar work, the agent draws on real historical data from their own completed projects. Not industry averages. Not an eight-year-old spreadsheet. Their actual costs, their actual crew performance, their actual material usage, in their actual market.

Every question is a graph traversal, not a pre-built report. "Which jobs made the most money this year, and why?" "Should I bid this hospital job?" "What is my risk exposure this week?" The agent traverses costs, delays, variations, crew productivity, certifications, weather, sub performance — and returns a narrative answer informed by both structured data and past conversation context.

**What the contractor does:** Asks questions. **What the agent does:** Traverses the connected graph across every dimension. Answers in plain language with the reasoning shown. Carries the learning forward into future estimates and decisions.

### 3.10 Cross-Cutting: Safety and Compliance

Safety is not a step in the lifecycle — it runs through every stage. The knowledge graph encodes regulatory requirements as traversable structure: which regulations apply in this jurisdiction, which activities require which certifications, which inspections are mandated, which training is due. The agent checks compliance continuously — not as a separate audit, but as a natural consequence of everything being connected.

When a worker is assigned to a task, the agent checks their certifications against the regulatory requirements for that activity in that jurisdiction. When equipment is scheduled, the agent checks maintenance and inspection status. When a new project starts, the agent generates the required safety documentation from the project scope and the jurisdiction's requirements.

Safety data enriches everything else. Time tracking connects to fatigue analysis. Daily logs surface incident patterns. Quality deficiencies correlate with schedule pressure. The morning brief incorporates risk scores, weather hazards, expiring certifications, open corrective actions, and schedule conflicts — assembled automatically from the graph.

### 3.11 Cross-Cutting: Subcontractor Management

When the contractor works as a sub (for a GC), Kerf manages the relationship: compliance documentation, insurance certificates, prequalification submissions, and performance tracking. When the contractor manages subs (as a GC), Kerf provides real-time visibility into every sub's compliance status, training currency, insurance validity, and performance metrics.

Insurance certificates are AI-parsed from uploaded documents — carrier, policy, limits, expiry dates extracted and monitored. The agent sends renewal reminders automatically and escalates if not renewed. Performance scoring draws from safety data, inspection results, and corrective action closure times.

### 3.12 Cross-Cutting: The Agent

The agent does not just inform — it acts, within boundaries the contractor controls:

- **Autonomous** (no approval needed): Send certification renewal reminders, check sub insurance status, generate reports, flag compliance issues, prepare morning briefs, detect potential variations in daily logs, monitor payment status, track weather against scheduled work
- **Propose and confirm** (contractor reviews before execution): Draft variations with evidence, suggest estimate adjustments based on historical data, recommend schedule changes, identify cost overruns with recommended actions, generate invoices, draft outbound client emails (proposals, replies to questions, variation explanations), propose new quote versions when clients request changes
- **Contractor approves** (consequential actions): Place material orders with suppliers, submit proposals, send invoices to clients, accept contract terms, modify schedules affecting other parties, send any client-facing email

The agent's memory accumulates over time. After a year, it knows the contractor's labour rates, crew productivity, GC relationships, supplier preferences, and decision-making patterns. Every conversation, every document uploaded, every job completed adds to the graph. The system that has seen 50 of your jobs knows your business better than any spreadsheet ever could.

### 3.13 Cross-Cutting: Voice and Mobile as the Primary Interface

Voice and mobile are not a companion modality to Kerf — they are the primary interface. The full product is usable from a phone, standing on a jobsite, with one hand free. Desktop is useful for review and analysis, but nothing in the core workflow requires it. For the solo electrician and the 20-person crew, the field IS the office, and the mobile surface IS the system of record. If a workflow only completes "back at the desk," it is broken for this segment.

**Walking estimate capture.** The contractor taps "start estimate" and walks the site narrating. Each utterance produces a provisional WorkItem card, timestamped to the audio segment it came from, with any photo taken in the same window attached automatically. The contractor corrects in real time — "no, 2 circuits not 3" — and the card updates. By the time the walk is over, the estimate is already structured. The proposal is in the client's inbox before the contractor is back in the truck.

**Learned-pattern prompting, not template prompting.** When the contractor says "200-amp panel upgrade," the agent does not consult a pre-authored template library asking for thirty parameters most of which are irrelevant. Instead, it looks at the contractor's own past panel upgrades via semantic similarity over their graph, and asks only about the parameters that have actually varied across those jobs. If every panel upgrade this contractor has done used the same meter main type, the agent does not ask. If distance-to-existing-panel varied from 8 to 40 feet and correlated with labour hours, that is the one parameter it prompts for. Kerf does not have a template library to author or maintain — patterns are derived from the contractor's own completed work. The system learns what questions matter from the contractor's data, not from a template author's guess.

**One utterance, many graph writes.** A single voice session on a site walk populates the daily log, the safety inspection, quality observations, time entries, and estimate progress simultaneously — because each extracted claim is a separate graph mutation. When the crew lead says "pour went fine this morning, 40 yards placed, noticed the rebar spacing on the west pier looks wider than spec," the agent generates a DailyLog entry, a QualityObservation linked to a CorrectiveAction, and potentially a variation draft — all from one sentence. One walk, many documents, zero forms. This is how the "documentation is a byproduct of doing the work" belief becomes concrete.

**Offline-first by architectural requirement.** Poor cell reception is the default on jobsites, not an edge case. Voice recordings, photos, and provisional graph writes queue locally on the device and reconcile on sync. This is a constraint from day one, not a Phase 2 feature — the contractor in a basement mechanical room or on a rural pole-barn slab cannot be told the app does not work there.

**Drawing and voice together where geometry matters.** For fencing, paving, decking, site concrete, and other geometric trades, drawing on a satellite overlay or a scale sketch is faster than counting by voice. Kerf pairs both: draw the shape on the map, narrate the spec — "galvanized chain link, 8-foot with barbed wire top." Neither modality is forced, and neither is treated as a second-class citizen.

### 3.14 Cross-Cutting: Email as a Client Channel

Kerf connects to the contractor's email (Gmail, Outlook, or equivalent via OAuth) and treats email as a first-class bidirectional channel with clients. Quotes, variation proposals, progress updates, invoices, and responses to client questions are drafted by the agent and sent from the contractor's own address — preserving the relationship continuity that a separate portal or no-reply address destroys.

Inbound handling is scoped to threads Kerf originated. The agent does not scan the contractor's general inbox; it only reads replies to emails it sent. Privacy model: tight.

When a client replies in a Kerf-originated thread, the agent reads the message, links it to the relevant Project in the graph, and proposes the next action:

- **Clarifying question** → draft a reply grounded in the contract, methodology, or historical context
- **Request for changes** → draft a new document version (Quote v2, revised invoice, amended variation) alongside a draft email explaining the change
- **Acceptance or approval** → propose the state transition (e.g., `QUOTED → ACCEPTED`) and the contract snapshot that locks with it
- **Objection or dispute signal** → flag explicitly, draft a measured response, never send without explicit approval

The contractor always approves before anything is sent. Email to clients carries legal and relationship consequences that belong in human hands. The magic-link portal remains the primary surface for structured actions (Accept, Decline, Sign); email is the conversational channel that carries the links and handles the human-language layer around them.

Every inbound and outbound email is stored in the graph — linked to the Project, timestamped, and indexed for semantic retrieval. "What did the client actually say about the pantry outlet?" becomes a one-query answer, not an inbox search.

### 3.15 Cross-Cutting: Conversation and Canvas

The primary way the contractor interacts with Kerf is **conversation** — chat, voice, or email. The primary way Kerf presents answers to open questions is **canvas** — a dynamic, LLM-generated visualisation rendered on demand from a graph traversal.

Traditional construction software is built around menus, dashboards, and pre-built reports. Every answer the contractor might want requires a developer to have anticipated the question, designed a view, and shipped it. Custom reports are the line where product teams drown.

Kerf inverts this. The contractor asks a question. The agent translates the question into a graph traversal, executes it, chooses the appropriate visualisation, and renders a canvas. No menu. No dashboard. No pre-built report. The next question pivots or refines the canvas in place.

**The canvas is not one thing — it is whatever the answer needs to be:**

- A **table** when the answer is rows ("which subs are most reliable?" → ranked table with reliability metrics, sortable)
- A **timeline** when the answer is a sequence ("walk me through the Peachtree job" → annotated timeline from bid to close with every decision linked to evidence)
- A **chart** when the answer is a trend ("is my margin improving?" → line chart by quarter, with crossover to estimating accuracy)
- A **map** when the answer is spatial ("where are my open hazards?" → site map with hazard pins and status colours)
- A **narrative** when the answer is causal ("why did we lose money on the hospital job?" → prose with inline evidence chain — time entries, material overages, delay days, missed change orders)
- A **comparison** when the answer is relative ("how does this bid compare to similar past jobs?" → side-by-side WorkItem comparison with variance highlighted)
- A **Gantt or kanban** when the answer is work-in-progress ("what's the week ahead look like?" → interactive schedule with drag-to-reschedule)
- A **forecast** when the answer is future-looking ("will I hit my quarterly target?" → pipeline plus historical conversion curve)
- A **document view** when the answer is a file ("show me the signed contract for this job" → PDF with the agent's annotations highlighting relevant clauses)

Every canvas is interactive. Clicking any element drills into the underlying graph. Asking a follow-up question refines or pivots the view. A canvas is a conversation continued in visual form, not a static report.

**Principles governing canvas:**

- **Any question produces a canvas.** There is no "this isn't a supported report" state. If the agent cannot answer, it says so in the conversation; it does not fail silently.
- **Every canvas is grounded in the graph.** No synthesised numbers. Every data point is traceable to its source node or nodes. Click to see the path.
- **The canvas follows the conversation.** It does not replace it. The contractor keeps typing or speaking; the canvas updates or pivots beneath.
- **Canvas is the right output for open questions; forms are the right output for structured data entry.** Canvas does not replace the inspection form or the estimate entry screen. It replaces the report, the dashboard, and the menu.

**Why this is cheap on a knowledge graph and hard for competitors to match:**

The canvas is cheap to ship on a knowledge graph because every node has typed properties, every relationship has semantics, and Cypher traversals map cleanly to the structure an LLM needs to pick a visualisation. The ontology is the visualisation grammar. A relational-database competitor cannot ship this without rebuilding — their schema was not designed for arbitrary traversal, and their UI was not designed for rendered-on-demand views. ## 4. THE INTELLIGENCE ARCHITECTURE

Kerf's intelligence comes from three complementary data layers, unified by the knowledge graph and accessed through the conversational interface.

### Layer 1: The Knowledge Graph (Neo4j)

**What lives here:** All structured entities and their relationships — projects, workers, estimates, tasks, time entries, daily logs, certifications, regulations, cost lines, change orders, contacts, equipment. Plus: decisions and insights extracted from conversations, regulatory rules encoded as traversable structure, requirements extracted from uploaded documents, and the ontology that connects everything.

**How agents use it:** Cypher traversal. Deterministic, auditable, citable. "Is this worker certified for this task?" returns the same answer every time, with a citation chain showing exactly which regulation requires which certification.

**This is the system of truth. It is also Kerf's core architectural commitment — the universal substrate on which every feature is built.**

Every feature in Kerf is a traversal of this graph, not a separate module with its own schema. New capabilities are built by extending the ontology and writing prompts, not by building new databases or new UI. A relational-database competitor adding an "AI Copilot" bolts LLMs onto tables — they get autocomplete and summarisation. Kerf's agent traverses a connected graph that includes operational data, financial data, regulatory structure, document extractions, and conversation history as one substrate. The difference is structural, not incremental. Every new entity the ontology grows makes every existing feature richer; every new feature unlocked is mostly a new query and a new prompt, not a new product.

### Layer 2: The Semantic Layer (Vector Index within Neo4j)

**What lives here:** Embeddings of everything unstructured — conversation transcripts, uploaded document text (specs, contracts, plans), photos with AI-generated descriptions, emails, PDF reports. Each embedding is stored as a native property on a graph node, linked to all related entities via graph relationships.

Neo4j 5.11+ supports vector embeddings as native node properties and HNSW vector indexes for similarity search. This is not a separate database — it is a built-in capability of the same graph database that stores all structured entities. A vector embedding is stored as a property on a Conversation node, a DocumentChunk node, or a Photo node, alongside all their other properties and graph relationships. This means hybrid queries — combining semantic similarity with graph traversal — execute in a single Cypher query against a single database. No separate vector store to sync, no data duplication, no consistency issues between systems.

**How agents use it:** Semantic similarity search for context retrieval. When the contractor asks "should I bid this school job?", the agent retrieves past conversations where they discussed institutional work, finds relevant spec sections from similar projects, and surfaces photo evidence from comparable jobs — all through semantic search, connected to the graph.

**This is the system of context.**

### Layer 3: The Document Store (Object Storage)

**What lives here:** Raw files at full fidelity — PDFs, photos, voice recordings, plan sheets, contracts, COIs, permits. Original format, permanently retained.

**How agents use it:** Retrieval by reference when the full document is needed. The agent can show the contractor the original plan sheet, the signed contract, or play back a voice recording.

**This is the system of record.**

### How Data Flows Through The Layers

**Conversation flow:**
1. Contractor chats or speaks to Kerf
2. Voice is transcribed to text (if voice input)
3. The conversation engine: understands intent, queries Layer 1 (graph) for structured answers, queries Layer 2 (vectors) for relevant context from past conversations and documents, executes actions, and responds conversationally
4. After the interaction: full transcript stored in Layer 3 (raw storage); vector embedding stored as a property on the Conversation node in Layer 2 (semantic search); extracted entities, decisions, and insights stored as graph nodes linked to projects, contacts, and other entities referenced in Layer 1

**Document upload flow:**
1. Contractor uploads a document against a project
2. Raw file stored in Layer 3 (object storage)
3. AI processing extracts structured data: plans yield fixture counts, dimensions, room layouts, and annotations as graph entities; contracts yield parties, scope, value, dates, and payment terms as graph entities; specs are chunked into sections with embeddings for semantic search; COIs yield carrier, policy number, limits, and expiry dates as graph entities with automated expiry monitoring; permits yield type, number, conditions, and required inspections as graph entities
4. Text content chunked and embedded as DocumentChunk nodes in Layer 2 (semantic search)
5. Structured data linked to project and related entities in Layer 1 (graph)

**Photo flow:**
1. Contractor takes a photo (or uploads one)
2. Raw image stored in Layer 3
3. AI generates description, detects objects (safety hazards, equipment, progress, defects) — stored as graph entities in Layer 1
4. Description embedded as a property on the Photo node in Layer 2 (semantic search)
5. GPS location, timestamp, project link stored as graph relationships in Layer 1

### Conversation Memory

Every conversation with Kerf produces a Conversation node in the graph, linked to everything it touches:

- **ABOUT_PROJECT** — which projects were discussed
- **ABOUT_ENTITY** — which workers, contacts, estimate lines, or other entities were referenced
- **REFERENCES** — which historical projects or past jobs were mentioned for comparison
- **PRODUCED_DECISION** — what decisions were made and why (stored as Decision nodes with description, reasoning, and alternatives considered)
- **EXPRESSED_KNOWLEDGE** — what institutional knowledge was shared (stored as Insight nodes with content, confidence, and applicability tags)
- **TRIGGERED_ACTION** — what entities were created, modified, or actioned during the conversation

The transcript text and its vector embedding enable semantic retrieval — "find conversations where we discussed renovation productivity." The graph links enable structured retrieval — "what decisions have we made about receptacle installation rates?" Together, they allow the agent to recall both what was said AND what it meant, in the context of everything else in the system.

**Retention:** Full transcripts retained 90 days in Layer 3. Extracted summaries, decisions, insights, and vector embeddings retained indefinitely in Layers 1 and 2. The contractor's institutional knowledge accumulates permanently in the graph.

## 6. PRODUCT PRINCIPLES

**1. Three taps or less for any field task.** If a contractor needs more than three taps to start a toolbox talk, log an inspection, clock in a worker, or submit a hazard report, we have failed. Complexity belongs on the server, not on the screen.

**2. If a contractor cannot finish it in two minutes, it does not ship.** Two minutes is the maximum window a contractor has between operational demands. Design every field interaction to fit inside that window.

**3. Every language is a first-class language.** We do not build in English and translate later. Every supported language is authored with the same rigour, reviewed by native speakers, and tested with users in that language. The knowledge graph is language-agnostic at the structural level — regulations, relationships, and rules are the same regardless of what language the user speaks. The conversational interface, safety documents, toolbox talks, and agent responses operate in the user's language natively. Initial markets: English + Spanish (US), English + Polish + Romanian (UK), English + French (Canada), English + Mandarin + Vietnamese (Australia).

**4. Every AI output must cite the specific standard.** We never say "you should use fall protection" without saying "per OSHA 1926.502(d)." Citations build trust, enable verification, and distinguish us from generic AI.

**5. We never require forms when conversation will do.** The contractor should be able to accomplish anything in Kerf by talking to it — chat or voice. Forms, dashboards, and structured interfaces exist as alternatives and for review, but the conversational interface is always the primary path. The jobsite is not an office. Hands are dirty, gloved, or holding tools. Conversation is the natural interface.

**6. The system gets smarter every day, but the user never gets dumber.** AI should augment judgment, not replace it. Every AI interaction is a micro-training opportunity.

**7. No surveillance, ever.** Kerf tracks safety activities, work time, and project status — not people. We do not build productivity monitoring or anything that could be used to discipline workers for non-safety, non-time reasons. Time tracking measures hours for payroll and cost allocation, not speed or output. Trust is non-negotiable.

**8. Offline-first for every field function.** Cell service on construction sites is unreliable. Every field function must work fully offline with automatic sync when connectivity returns.

**9. We show the ROI in dollars, not dashboards.** Contractors think in dollars. "Your compliance score improved 15 points" means nothing. "Your EMR is projected to drop from 1.12 to 0.97, saving you $38,000 in premiums and qualifying you for 12 additional projects" means everything.

**10. The best documentation is the documentation that writes itself.** The end state is not a better form — it is no form. The toolbox talk record comes from the talk. The inspection log comes from the walkthrough. The daily log comes from the safety walk plus a 2-minute narrative. The timesheet comes from GPS and clock-in taps. The estimate comes from walking the site and talking. Every manual data entry we eliminate is a step toward documentation as a byproduct of work.

**11. One tool, not six.** Every feature we add must integrate with existing data rather than creating a new silo. The daily log auto-populates from safety data. The schedule connects to certifications. Time tracking connects to incident analysis and job costing. The estimate becomes the task list becomes the cost report. If a new feature does not get better by being in Kerf alongside everything else, it does not belong in Kerf.

**12. The estimate is the task list is the cost report.** One data structure, three lifecycle stages. We never ask the contractor to enter the same information twice. An estimate line item becomes a schedulable task the moment the bid is won, and a cost tracking line the moment work begins.

**13. Conversational first. Chat always. Voice everywhere.** The primary interface to Kerf is a conversation — the contractor tells Kerf what they need, and Kerf understands, acts, and responds. Chat is the foundation that works in every context. Voice is the field-optimised layer on top. Both are always available. Every conversation is recorded, linked, and available for future reference. The contractor who talks to Kerf for a year has an intelligent assistant that knows their business, their preferences, their history, and their patterns.

**14. Every interaction makes the system smarter.** Every conversation, every document uploaded, every photo taken, every estimate created, every job completed adds to the knowledge graph. The system that has seen 50 of your jobs knows your business better than a spreadsheet that has seen zero. This is not a feature — it is the fundamental architecture. Intelligence accumulates because the graph connects everything.

**15. Advise, highlight, keep logical, do what you're told.** The contractor controls state transitions, and every other consequential action. Kerf advises with context, highlights issues and risks, enforces only those preconditions where a transition is logically impossible without them (e.g. Practical Completion with WorkItems below 100%), and then does what the operator asks. The agent never blocks on inferred facts, never auto-transitions based on silence or elapsed time, and never overrides contractor judgment with "best practice." It surfaces nudges; the operator decides. The operator is the expert; the agent is the assistant. This is how the contractor stays in control of their business.

They have budget for Procore and Intelex; they have dedicated estimators, schedulers, and safety directors; they run country-specific subsidiaries on country-specific stacks. Every one of Kerf's differentiators — voice-first, one-tool-not-six, safety-first architecture, contractor-own-data learning, conversation-and-canvas — weakens at enterprise scale where roles are specialised and workflows are process-heavy. Chasing enterprise would distort our product and distract us from the 750,000 small contractors who have nothing.

**We will not build worker performance tracking or disciplinary tools.** Kerf will never include features that allow managers to score, rank, or discipline individual workers based on safety or time data. Time tracking measures hours for payroll, not speed or output. The moment workers believe the tool is being used to evaluate their performance, honest reporting dies.

**We will not build our own hardware or wearables.** The phone in the worker's pocket is sufficient for every function we need.

**We will not build full CPM scheduling.** We are not competing with Primavera. We build lookahead scheduling — the 2-4 week rolling view that contractors actually use daily — connected to safety data, certifications, and equipment availability. If a contractor needs critical path scheduling with earned value analysis, they use Oracle. If they need to see what is happening this week on their phone, they use Kerf.

**We will not build trade-specific estimating databases.** We do not maintain catalogues of electrical assemblies, concrete mix specifications, or demolition cost-per-square-foot databases. The contractor's own historical data in the graph — their actual labour rates, their real material costs, their crew productivity on similar jobs — is more accurate than any industry database. The graph learns from the contractor's own work. Third-party cost data (RSMeans, supplier APIs) can be integrated as reference points, not as the primary pricing source.

**We will not replace QuickBooks.** We do not build general ledger, tax preparation, payroll processing, or accounts receivable management. QuickBooks handles accounting. Kerf handles intelligence — the connected understanding of why jobs make or lose money, which the accounting system cannot provide. Financial data syncs between the two.

### Year 1.5: The Graph Intelligence Advantage

As the knowledge graph accumulates operational and financial data plus hundreds of hours of conversation context per contractor, a structural advantage emerges that no relational-database competitor can replicate.

Competitors built on relational databases (JobTread, Buildertrend, PlanRadar, Fieldwire) can add AI features — and they will. But their AI reads tables. Kerf's AI traverses a connected graph that includes not just structured data but the reasoning behind every decision, the context from every uploaded document, and the institutional knowledge from every conversation. The difference: their AI can tell you what happened. Kerf's AI can tell you why, what it means, and what to do about it.

This advantage compounds. Every job estimated, tracked, and completed adds to the graph. After 50 jobs, the agent knows the contractor's business better than the contractor does. No spreadsheet, no relational database, and no bolt-on AI can replicate a knowledge graph that has been accumulating connected operational intelligence for two years.

### Year 2: Data Compound Effects

With thousands of customers generating daily operational data across safety, quality, time, financial performance, and scheduling, Kerf's predictive models see patterns no individual company or single-feature tool could detect. The safety-time correlation (fatigue predicts incidents). The quality-schedule correlation (rushed work produces deficiencies). The sub-compliance-performance correlation (declining inspection scores predict incidents AND budget overruns). The estimate-accuracy correlation (jobs estimated conversationally with historical data outperform spreadsheet estimates by 15%+). The morning brief is powered by millions of real data points and thousands of real contractor conversations, not generic advice.

The GC/sub network and insurance carrier integrations create multi-sided switching costs. A sub uses Kerf because three GCs require it. A GC requires Kerf because 60% of their subs already use it. An insurance carrier integrates because thousands of policyholders are on the platform. Each participant's value increases and switching cost increases as the network grows.

### Year 5: Global Industry Infrastructure

Kerf has become the default operating system for small construction companies — first in the United States, then the UK, Canada, Australia, and beyond. The knowledge graph encodes regulatory requirements across jurisdictions. The conversational interface operates in a dozen languages. The data flowing through the platform is the most comprehensive real-time picture of construction operations in the industry. The moat is not any single thing — it is the compounding of data, conversation intelligence, network, workflow embedding, and ecosystem integration over time.

## 12. THE ENDGAME

If Kerf achieves its full vision, the construction industry in 2035 looks fundamentally different.

The fatality rate for small contractors drops below the large-contractor rate for the first time in recorded history — not because small contractors hired safety directors, but because the AI safety director made the same expertise available to a 15-person framing crew as a 5,000-person general contractor.

The average contractor uses one app instead of six. The paper timesheet is dead. The Sunday-night paperwork session is dead. The kitchen-table estimating session is dead. The three-ring binder is dead. Documentation happens as a byproduct of doing the work, not as a separate activity that steals time from the work itself. The contractor talks to Kerf, and Kerf handles the rest.

The contractor who used to guess on bids now knows — from their own historical data in the graph — what their jobs actually cost, which crews are productive on which types of work, and where their estimates consistently miss. The change orders that used to get eaten are captured automatically. The job that used to lose money without anyone noticing until it was over is flagged in week two.

Insurance underwriting transforms. Instead of lagging indicators, carriers underwrite based on real-time, verified operational data — safety performance, quality deficiency rates, time-on-site patterns, financial performance, and sub compliance scores. Contractors who invest in doing things right see immediate premium reductions.

The relationship between general contractors and subcontractors around compliance shifts from adversarial paperwork exchange to real-time transparency. GCs stop demanding binders. Subs stop fabricating documentation. The pretence that paper compliance equals real safety dies because real-time data makes pretence unnecessary and detectable.

The multilingual gap closes. Construction workers around the world receive safety information, daily instructions, and operational tools in their language, every day.

And the individual human impact — the part that does not show up in market analyses — is that thousands of workers who would have been injured go home safe. Not because their employers suddenly cared more, but because the system made it harder to miss the danger and easier to do the right thing.

Construction is the most dangerous major industry in the world. More workers die building things than in almost any other sector. That fact has been constant for decades. We do not accept it. We believe one tool — precise, practical, conversational, and built for the people who actually build things — can change it.

That is the endgame. Not a software company. A safer, better-run industry.
