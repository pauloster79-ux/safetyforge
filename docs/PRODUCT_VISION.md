## 1. THE WORLD WE'RE BUILDING TOWARD

The construction industry has changed — not in the way the trade magazines predicted, with drones and robots and 3D-printed houses, but in the way that actually matters: the 30-person crew that used to juggle six apps, three binders, and a filing cabinet now runs everything through one tool. Safety, daily logs, time tracking, quality inspections, sub compliance, scheduling — all of it, in one app that speaks the language of the jobsite.

The change did not come from enterprise software trickling down to small contractors. Procore still costs $30,000 a year, and the 50-person GC in Texas still cannot justify it. The change came from a platform that started with the hardest, most regulated part of construction operations — safety compliance — and expanded outward, using the same data, the same inspection engine, and the same voice-first field experience to handle everything a superintendent touches in a day.

Marco Gutierrez runs a concrete crew of 22 in Phoenix. Three years ago, his morning looked like this: check the weather on his phone, open one app for the safety checklist, open another for the daily log, open a third for time tracking, open email for RFI responses, check a spreadsheet for the schedule, and spend Sunday night reconciling it all. His safety program was a binder on a shelf. His daily logs were fiction written from memory. His timesheets were paper. His punch lists were sticky notes.

Today, Marco opens Kerf at 5:30 AM. The morning brief is waiting — risk score, weather, expiring certifications, yesterday's open corrective actions, today's schedule, and a toolbox talk ready in English and Spanish. He delivers the talk to his crew (they sign off on their phones). He walks the site with his phone recording — the AI extracts the safety inspection, quality observations, crew count, equipment status, and weather conditions from his narration and photos. His daily log is 80% pre-filled from data the system already captured. He reviews it, adds the work-completed narrative, and taps submit. His crew clocked in via GPS when they arrived. The weekly lookahead schedule shows the forming crew finishing Thursday and the pour on Friday — and Kerf flagged that two workers' fall protection certifications expire Wednesday, so they need renewal before the pour.

For Marco, the change is not that he has better software. The change is that he has one tool instead of six, and that tool does the thinking. His EMR is 0.78. His insurance premiums dropped $52,000 last year. He won three bids he would have been disqualified from in 2026. His crew has gone 847 days without a lost-time incident. And he spends less time on paperwork now than he did when he was faking it.

For Sarah Chen, who owns a 45-person electrical contracting firm in Atlanta, the change means she got her Sundays back. She used to spend four hours every weekend reconciling inspection logs, updating training records, reviewing timesheets, chasing sub insurance certificates, and generating the documentation her GCs demanded. Now she spends twenty minutes reviewing a dashboard that already has the answers. Her prequalification submissions, which used to consume three full days twice a year, now take an afternoon — the system pre-fills 80% from data it already has. When a GC asks for her sub compliance status, she shares a link. When an RFI comes in, it goes into the log, gets assigned, and has a due date — no more lost emails.

For the industry, the change is structural. Insurance carriers have granular real-time data from small contractors — not annual self-reported summaries, but daily inspection results, time-on-site records, quality deficiency rates, incident reports, and compliance scores. General contractors can see every subcontractor's real-time compliance, training currency, and performance metrics. And for the first time, the 2-3 million Spanish-speaking construction workers in the United States receive safety information, daily instructions, and operational tools in their language, at their literacy level, every single day.

This is the world Kerf exists to build. Not a world where construction paperwork is easier to fill out, but a world where documentation is a natural byproduct of doing the work. Not a world with a better safety app, a better daily log app, and a better time tracker, but a world where the superintendent opens one tool in the morning and everything they need is there.

Eleven hundred people died on construction sites in the United States last year. Most of them worked for companies with fewer than 50 employees. Most of those companies had no safety director, incomplete daily logs, paper timesheets, and no affordable way to change any of it. We are building the thing that changes that.

## 2. OUR BELIEFS

**We believe safety is the foundation, not a silo.** Safety touches everything on a construction site — scheduling, workforce, equipment, quality, compliance, costs. A platform that starts with safety and expands outward has a natural architecture, because the safety data enriches every other feature. An inspection engine that does safety also does quality. A worker record that tracks certifications also tracks time. A daily log that captures safety observations also captures production progress. Safety is the connective tissue of construction operations, and we build from that centre.

**We believe documentation should be a byproduct of doing the work, not a separate activity.** If a foreman has to stop building to start documenting, we have failed. The inspection log should come from the inspection, not from a memory of the inspection four days later. The daily log should come from the daily work, not from Sunday-night catch-up sessions. The toolbox talk record should come from the toolbox talk, not from a foreman scribbling names on a sign-in sheet after the crew has already walked away.

**We believe every worker deserves information in their language, regardless of their employer's budget.** A laborer who speaks Spanish should not receive worse safety communication, weaker training, or less access to operational tools than a laborer who speaks English. Bilingual support is not a premium feature or a Phase 2 addition — it is a fundamental obligation.

**We believe the foreman is the most important user in construction technology, and the most ignored.** Every construction software product is built for the office. The foreman — the person who actually manages the crew, the site, and the risk — is an afterthought forced to use tools designed for someone sitting at a desk. We build for the foreman first. If it does not work in the field, it does not work.

**We believe small contractors are not less safe because they care less — they are less safe because they have less help.** The owner of a 25-person roofing company is not cutting corners out of malice. She is cutting corners because she is also the estimator, the project manager, the HR department, and the accounts receivable clerk. She has fifteen minutes a week for safety, and the compliance system demands forty hours. We close that gap.

**We believe AI in construction must be auditable, cited, and humble.** Every recommendation Kerf makes must trace back to a specific standard, regulation, or data point. We never present AI judgment as authoritative truth. We show our work. We tell users when we are uncertain. We make it easy to verify. The cost of a wrong answer in this domain is not a bad search result — it is a person going home in an ambulance.

**We believe compliance should be a competitive advantage, not just a cost of doing business.** Better compliance leads to lower EMR, lower premiums, more bid eligibility, and more revenue. Safety should make contractors money, and we prove it.

**We believe the phone in the foreman's pocket is the most powerful tool on the jobsite.** More powerful than any sensor, wearable, or IoT device. It has a camera, a microphone, GPS, internet access, and it is already there. We do not need new hardware. We need software that uses the hardware construction workers already carry.

**We believe one tool is better than six.** Construction workers average 6.2 separate technology products. Each one adds cognitive load, data silos, and adoption friction. The superintendent who does their safety walk, daily log, time check, quality inspection, and schedule review in one app will never go back to five. Consolidation is not a feature — it is the product.

## 3. THE FOUR HORIZONS

### Horizon 1: The AI Safety Director — Voice-First, Day One (Months 1-6)

This is where Kerf earns its first dollar and its first trust — not as a form-filler, but as a fundamentally different way to manage construction safety.

A contractor finds us because they searched "OSHA fall protection written program" at 11 PM on a Sunday night. They land on our site, run the free OSHA readiness quiz, and get a compliance score of 34 out of 100. They sign up for the free trial. Within ten minutes, they have generated their first written safety program — a Fall Protection Plan customized to their company, their trade, their project types, and the specific OSHA standards that apply. It is not a template with blanks. It is a complete, professional document with their company information woven throughout, with the correct regulatory citations, with site-specific procedures they can actually follow.

The next morning, their foreman opens the app on his phone. There is a toolbox talk ready in English and Spanish. He gathers the crew, delivers the talk, the crew signs off on their phones. Then he does his morning site walk — presses the record button and talks as he walks, taking photos as he goes. Kerf transcribes everything, extracts the structured inspection data, links the photos to findings, flags corrective actions, and presents the complete report. The foreman taps confirm. Done in four minutes, voice and photos only. No forms. No typing. No checkboxes.

By the end of the first week, the daily routine is established. The owner opens her dashboard Friday afternoon and sees, for the first time in her career, an honest picture of her compliance status across all projects. Not what she hopes is happening. What is actually happening.

This is what Horizon 1 delivers. The foundation on which everything else is built.

### Horizon 2: The Construction Operations Platform (Months 6-12)

This is where Kerf stops being a safety tool and starts being the superintendent's operating system.

**Daily Logs.** The superintendent doing a safety walk is already observing weather, manpower, work progress, and equipment status. Kerf captures 40-50% of the daily log through the safety workflow. Adding the work-completed narrative, materials received, and delay documentation turns the safety walk into a complete daily report — the most legally important document a contractor produces. Auto-populated from data the system already has, the superintendent adds only what is new. What used to take 30 minutes of writing after a long day now takes a 5-minute review.

**Quality Inspections and Punch Lists.** The inspection engine already exists. A quality inspection is the same workflow as a safety inspection: walk the site, observe, photograph, note deficiencies, assign corrective actions, track to closure. Different templates, same code. The superintendent who does one walk produces both the safety report and the quality report. No double entry. No second app.

**Time Tracking.** Worker records with project assignments already exist. Toolbox talk attendance is already an implicit check-in. Adding GPS-verified clock-in/clock-out and cost code allocation solves a top-3 pain point — 38% of contractors still use paper timesheets, and bad time tracking costs ~$52K/year for a 50-person crew. The unique advantage: no competitor connects time data to safety data. Kerf can flag "Worker X has been on site 11 hours — fatigue-related incident risk elevated" because it has both datasets.

**Expanded Equipment Management.** Already tracking equipment inspections and DOT compliance. Add maintenance scheduling, utilisation tracking, and per-project cost allocation.

**Expanded Subcontractor Management.** Already have the GC portal with prequalification automation. Add automated COI tracking (7 out of 10 certificates of insurance are non-compliant), insurance expiry alerts, performance scoring from existing safety data, and lien waiver management.

### Horizon 3: The Project Coordination Layer (Months 12-18)

This is where Kerf becomes the platform that replaces Procore for the $5-50M GC.

**RFIs and Submittals.** A structured log with assignments, due dates, ball-in-court tracking, and audit trails. The MVP is straightforward — the same create/assign/track/close workflow used for inspections and corrective actions. For a 50-person GC currently using email and Excel for RFIs, this eliminates the $859K average RFI friction cost per project.

**Basic Scheduling.** Not Primavera. A weekly lookahead — a rolling 2-4 week task board that the superintendent can manage from their phone. Which crews are where, what is coming up, where are the bottlenecks. Connected to safety data: if a crane operator's certification expires Tuesday and they are scheduled for Wednesday, Kerf flags the conflict before it becomes a compliance violation.

**Change Order Tracking.** Connected to RFIs and project records. Scope change documentation with cost and schedule impact.

### Horizon 4: The Construction Intelligence Platform (Year 2-3)

This is where Kerf becomes something no competitor can replicate, because the product's value comes not just from its software but from its data.

With thousands of customers generating daily operational data — voice recordings, video walkthroughs, inspections, incidents, quality deficiencies, time records, equipment utilisation, sub performance — Kerf's predictive models see patterns invisible to any individual company. The system knows that concrete contractors in the Southeast have a 340% spike in heat-related incidents during the third week of June. It knows that fall incidents on residential roofing projects increase 28% on the second day after rain. It knows that subs with declining inspection scores have a 4.2x higher incident rate in the following quarter. These patterns flow back into every customer's morning brief, schedule suggestions, and risk assessments.

The GC/sub network has transformed how compliance flows through the industry. Insurance carriers have integrated directly. Prequalification has become invisible. And the AI Safety Coach has emerged — daily, contextual, two-minute micro-training that builds real knowledge over time.

## 5. PRODUCT PRINCIPLES

**1. Three taps or less for any field task.** If a foreman needs more than three taps to start a toolbox talk, log an inspection, clock in a worker, or submit a hazard report, we have failed. Complexity belongs on the server, not on the screen.

**2. If a foreman cannot finish it in two minutes, it does not ship.** Two minutes is the maximum window a foreman has between operational demands. Design every field interaction to fit inside that window.

**3. Spanish is not a translation — it is a first-class language.** We do not build in English and translate later. Spanish content is authored with the same rigour, reviewed by native speakers, and tested with Spanish-speaking users. A Spanish-speaking user should never feel like they are using a translated product.

**4. Every AI output must cite the specific standard.** We never say "you should use fall protection" without saying "per OSHA 1926.502(d)." Citations build trust, enable verification, and distinguish us from generic AI.

**5. We never require typing when voice or photo will do.** The jobsite is not an office. Hands are dirty, gloved, or holding tools. Voice and camera are the primary input methods.

**6. The system gets smarter every day, but the user never gets dumber.** AI should augment judgment, not replace it. Every AI interaction is a micro-training opportunity.

**7. No surveillance, ever.** Kerf tracks safety activities, work time, and project status — not people. We do not build productivity monitoring or anything that could be used to discipline workers for non-safety, non-time reasons. Time tracking measures hours for payroll and cost allocation, not speed or output. Trust is non-negotiable.

**8. Offline-first for every field function.** Cell service on construction sites is unreliable. Every field function must work fully offline with automatic sync when connectivity returns.

**9. We show the ROI in dollars, not dashboards.** Contractors think in dollars. "Your compliance score improved 15 points" means nothing. "Your EMR is projected to drop from 1.12 to 0.97, saving you $38,000 in premiums and qualifying you for 12 additional projects" means everything.

**10. The best documentation is the documentation that writes itself.** The end state is not a better form — it is no form. The toolbox talk record comes from the talk. The inspection log comes from the walkthrough. The daily log comes from the safety walk plus a 2-minute narrative. The timesheet comes from GPS and clock-in taps. Every manual data entry we eliminate is a step toward documentation as a byproduct of work.

**11. One tool, not six.** Every feature we add must integrate with existing data rather than creating a new silo. The daily log auto-populates from safety data. The schedule connects to certifications. Time tracking connects to incident analysis. If a new feature does not get better by being in Kerf alongside everything else, it does not belong in Kerf.

## 6. THE EXPERIENCE VISION

### A Full Day with Kerf (at Full Maturity)

**5:30 AM — Marco's Kitchen, Phoenix, AZ**

Marco's phone buzzes. He opens Kerf while the coffee brews. The Morning Brief is waiting.

Today's risk score is 8.4 out of 10 — elevated. Temperature will hit 109 by 2 PM, triggering the heat illness prevention protocol. Javier's silica competent person certification expired — he cannot supervise cutting operations until it is renewed. The concrete supplier confirmed delivery at 7 AM; the pump truck will operate near overhead power lines on the south side. The schedule shows the forming crew finishing today and the pour tomorrow — assuming the certification issue is resolved. Kerf has preloaded a toolbox talk on heat illness prevention and generated a task-specific JHA for the pour that includes the power line proximity hazard. Yesterday's daily log flagged a delivery delay on rebar — Marco checks the schedule impact: no critical path issue yet, but the buffer is tight.

Marco taps "View Toolbox Talk." He scans it — good, covers hydration schedules, shade break requirements, and signs of heat stroke. Both English and Spanish versions are ready. He notices the JHA flagged the power line and recommends a spotter. He texts his superintendent. Four minutes, everything covered.

**6:00 AM — Jobsite**

The crew arrives. They clock in on their phones — one tap, GPS-verified. Kerf records their arrival, logs them to today's project, and starts their time against the correct cost codes. Carlos, who reads limited English, clocks in on the Spanish interface.

Marco delivers the toolbox talk. The crew signs off on their phones with a tap. Attendance, time verification, and safety training are all captured in one workflow.

**6:15 AM — The Morning Walk**

Marco clips his phone to his vest and presses record. He walks the site for eight minutes, narrating as he goes, pointing his camera at anything notable. He covers the pump truck clearance from power lines, shoring in the trench, guardrails on the east wall, fire extinguisher status, the quality of yesterday's pour finish on the north wall, a formwork alignment that needs adjustment, debris near the stairwell, and the crew count across two sub areas.

Eight minutes later, he stops recording. Kerf processes everything:
- **Safety inspection:** 14 items checked, 2 deficiencies flagged (debris, formwork alignment), corrective actions assigned
- **Quality observations:** Pour finish acceptable, formwork alignment corrective action created and assigned to the forming crew lead
- **Daily log draft:** Weather (clear, 92°F at 6:15 AM, forecast 109°F), crew count (18 own + 6 sub), equipment on site (pump truck, 2 forklifts, crane), safety observations, quality notes — all pre-filled
- Marco adds the work-completed narrative: "Completed column forms grid C1-C4. Stripped east wall forms. Pump truck positioned for tomorrow's pour." Reviews the draft. Two taps to confirm.

One site walk. Three reports. No forms.

**9:45 AM — Hazard Observation**

A delivery truck clips a temporary power pole. Carlos holds up his phone, takes a photo, presses the hazard button, and speaks in Spanish: "The delivery truck hit the power pole. It is leaning over the walkway." Kerf transcribes, translates, analyses the photo, classifies it as an imminent hazard, and pushes an alert to Marco. The hazard report is auto-generated — photo, voice note, AI assessment, GPS location, timestamp. A worker who might never have said anything just prevented a fall. Forty-five seconds.

**12:00 PM — Heat Check**

The temperature crosses 105. Kerf sends an automatic reminder: mandatory shade break. During the break, Marco checks the schedule — the afternoon plan was concrete finishing, but the heat protocol shortens the work window. He drags the remaining tasks to tomorrow on the lookahead board.

**2:30 PM — Incident**

A worker twists his ankle stepping off a form. Marco voice-dictates the incident report. Kerf asks clarifying questions, classifies it as first-aid (not recordable), generates the report, creates a corrective action, and cross-references with the time tracking data — the worker had been on site for 8.5 hours, within normal range, but notes that this is the second soft-tissue injury in the dismount area this month, suggesting a systemic issue. Marco assigns a corrective action to grade and compact all dismount areas.

**4:30 PM — End of Day**

Marco opens Kerf one last time. The end-of-day summary: toolbox talk completed, daily inspection completed, quality inspection completed, daily log filed, one hazard reported and resolved, one incident (first aid), 18 timesheets auto-verified. Tomorrow's brief is already being prepared. The weekly schedule lookahead shows Friday's pour is on track if the certification renewal happens Wednesday. No paperwork to carry home.

**6:30 PM — Sarah's Home Office**

Sarah opens the Kerf dashboard. Six projects, all visible. Four are green. One is yellow — the framing project has missing daily logs for two days. One is red — the new project's site-specific safety plan is due Friday. She taps on the red project; Kerf has already drafted the plan from the project scope and her existing programs. She reviews, makes two edits, approves for GC submission.

She checks the sub compliance dashboard. Three subs across her projects — two are green, one is amber (workers' comp certificate expires in 12 days). Kerf has already sent the automated reminder to the sub. She checks the weekly time summary — all timesheets are submitted, no discrepancies flagged.

She runs the monthly mock OSHA inspection. Score: 87 out of 100, up from 71 three months ago. Two findings, both minor. She assigns corrective actions.

She generates the quarterly insurance package: OSHA 300 summary, incident trends, training compliance, inspection completion rates, quality deficiency rates, corrective action closure times, time-on-site data, and current mock inspection score. The system projects her EMR will drop to 0.89 at the next rating period — saving an estimated $44,000 annually. She forwards the package to her broker with one tap.

Sunday arrives. Sarah is not in her home office. She is at the park with her kids. The paperwork did itself.

## 8. WHAT WE WON'T BUILD

**We will not build a template library or compete on template breadth.** We generate complete, site-specific, trade-specific, OSHA-referenced documents from project context. The AI IS the template. Adding a template library would signal that we are the same category of tool as SafetyCulture. We are not.

**We will not build a general-purpose inspection platform.** We do construction inspections — safety and quality — backed by OSHA intelligence and building code awareness, captured through voice and video. If someone asks for a restaurant health inspection template, the answer is no.

**We will not build a safety training LMS.** We deliver micro-training through the AI Safety Coach — contextual, two-minute interactions tied to the day's work. We do not build a full learning management system with courses, quizzes, and SCORM compliance.

**We will not chase enterprise accounts.** A Fortune 500 GC with 200 projects and a full safety department is not our customer. They have budget for Procore and Intelex. Serving them would distort our product and distract us from the 750,000 small contractors who have nothing.

**We will not build worker performance tracking or disciplinary tools.** Kerf will never include features that allow managers to score, rank, or discipline individual workers based on safety or time data. Time tracking measures hours for payroll, not speed or output. The moment workers believe the tool is being used to evaluate their performance, honest reporting dies.

**We will not build our own hardware or wearables.** The phone in the worker's pocket is sufficient for every function we need.

**We will not build full CPM scheduling.** We are not competing with Primavera. We build lookahead scheduling — the 2-4 week rolling view that superintendents actually use daily — connected to safety data, certifications, and equipment availability. If a contractor needs critical path scheduling with earned value analysis, they use Oracle. If they need to see what is happening this week on their phone, they use Kerf.

**We will not build estimating or bidding tools.** The preconstruction phase is a different product with different users (estimators, not field crews). We integrate with estimating tools through data exchange. We do not replicate them.

**We will not build full financial management.** We track time for payroll export and cost code allocation. We do not do invoicing, accounts receivable, general ledger, or financial reporting. QuickBooks exists. We integrate with it.

Instead of lagging indicators, carriers underwrite based on real-time, verified operational data — safety performance, quality deficiency rates, time-on-site patterns, and sub compliance scores. Contractors who invest in doing things right see immediate premium reductions.

The relationship between general contractors and subcontractors around compliance shifts from adversarial paperwork exchange to real-time transparency. GCs stop demanding binders. Subs stop fabricating documentation. The pretence that paper compliance equals real safety dies because real-time data makes pretence unnecessary and detectable.

The multilingual gap closes. The 2-3 million Spanish-speaking construction workers receive safety information, daily instructions, and operational tools in their language, every day.

And the individual human impact — the part that does not show up in market analyses — is that thousands of workers who would have been injured go home safe. Not because their employers suddenly cared more, but because the system made it harder to miss the danger and easier to do the right thing.

Construction is the most dangerous major industry in the United States. More workers die building things than in any other sector. That fact has been constant for decades. We do not accept it. That is the endgame. Not a software company. A safer, better-run industry.
