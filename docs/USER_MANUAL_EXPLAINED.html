Kerf User Manual

Kerf is a construction safety management platform that helps contractors, general contractors, and subcontractors stay compliant with safety regulations, manage worker certifications, conduct inspections, generate safety documents, and track incidents. This manual explains every feature in the context of the real-world business processes and regulations that make them necessary.

Who This Manual Is For

This guide is written for someone with no prior construction industry experience. Every section explains the underlying business problem, the regulatory requirement that creates it, the traditional way companies handle it, and then how Kerf automates or simplifies the process. If you already work in construction safety, you can skip the business-context paragraphs and focus on the "How Kerf Helps" subsections.

Jurisdictions Overview

Construction safety regulation is not global. Each country has its own enforcement agency, laws, penalty structure, and terminology. Kerf supports four jurisdictions and adapts its forms, document templates, terminology, and compliance checks to match the regulatory framework of the selected jurisdiction.

🇺🇸United States
Agency: OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration)
Primary law: 29 CFR 1926 (Construction Industry Standards)
Recordkeeping: 29 CFR 1904 (OSHA 300 Log)
Enforcement: Federal OSHA + 22 State Plan states (e.g., Cal/OSHA, WA L&I)
Penalty range: Up to $16,131/serious violation; $161,323/willful or repeat
Key concept: Multi-employer doctrine (GCs can be cited for subcontractor failures)
🇬🇧United Kingdom
Agency: HSE (Health and Safety Executive)
Primary law: Health and Safety at Work Act 1974
Construction-specific: CDM Regulations 2015 (Construction Design and Management)
Incident reporting: RIDDOR (Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations 2013)
Substances: COSHH Regulations 2002
Key concept: RAMS (Risk Assessments and Method Statements) are required before any work begins
🇦🇺Australia
Agency: Safe Work Australia (national); SafeWork NSW, WorkSafe Victoria, etc. (state)
Primary law: WHS Act 2011 (harmonised nationally, except Victoria and WA have their own versions)
Construction-specific: SWMS (Safe Work Method Statements) required for high-risk construction work
Threshold: WHS Management Plans for projects over $250,000
Key concept: Notifiable incidents must be reported to the regulator immediately by phone
🇨🇦Canada
Agency: Federal: Employment and Social Development Canada. Provincial: e.g., Ontario MOL, WorkSafeBC, Alberta OHS
Primary law: Canada Labour Code (federal); each province has separate OHS legislation
Construction-specific: FLHA (Field Level Hazard Assessment) widely used in Alberta/BC
Key concept: JHSC (Joint Health and Safety Committee) requirements; CSA standards referenced
Workers' comp: WSIB (Ontario), WorkSafeBC (British Columbia), etc.
Why jurisdictions matter in Kerf When you select a jurisdiction (via the dropdown in Settings or during company onboarding), Kerf adjusts document templates, inspection checklists, terminology (e.g., "OSHA 300 Log" vs. "RIDDOR Report"), penalty references, and compliance thresholds. This means the safety documents you generate are tailored to the laws that actually apply to your worksite.

Dashboard

Dashboard — Your Safety Command Center
The Business Need

A construction company's safety director needs to see the health of all projects at a glance every morning. Are inspections happening? Are any workers' certifications about to expire? Have there been recent incidents? Is our injury rate trending up? Without a centralized view, this information lives in scattered spreadsheets, filing cabinets, and different people's heads.

Regulatory Landscape

While no regulation specifically requires a "dashboard," many require proactive monitoring of safety programs. For example:

  • US OSHA's General Duty Clause (Section 5(a)(1)) requires employers to maintain a workplace free of recognized hazards. This effectively mandates ongoing safety oversight.
  • UK CDM 2015 requires the principal contractor to plan, manage, and monitor safety throughout the project lifecycle.
  • AU WHS Act 2011 requires PCBUs (Persons Conducting a Business or Undertaking) to ensure worker health and safety so far as is reasonably practicable, which implies active monitoring.
  • CA Provincial OHS legislation requires employers to establish and maintain a safety management system, which includes monitoring metrics like injury rates.
Traditional Approach

Safety directors typically start each day by making phone calls to site supervisors, reviewing hand-written inspection logs, checking a spreadsheet for upcoming certification expirations, and manually calculating injury rates from paper incident reports. This process is slow, error-prone, and often means problems are discovered after they have already become serious.

How Kerf Helps

The Kerf dashboard presents everything at a glance:

  • Status strip at the top shows five key numbers: Active Projects, Inspections completed, total Workers, Certification Alerts (expiring soon), and your company's TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate).
  • Document usage counter shows how many AI-generated documents you have used this month versus your plan's limit (e.g., "8 of 25 documents used").
  • Recent documents lists your latest safety documents with their status (DRAFT or FINAL) for quick access.
  • Certification alerts table highlights workers whose certifications are expiring within 30, 14, 7, or 1 day, color-coded by urgency.
  • Active projects shows each project with a compliance percentage ring, giving you an immediate sense of which projects need attention.
  • Quick actions: Three buttons let you jump directly to creating a New Inspection, New Document, or New Project.
  • Language toggle: Switch between English and Spanish (EN/ES) for the entire interface, important on bilingual construction sites.
Key Benefits
  • Reduces morning safety review from 30+ minutes to under 2 minutes
  • Ensures no expiring certification goes unnoticed, preventing workers from operating with lapsed credentials
  • TRIR visibility enables proactive intervention before rates climb to levels that affect insurance premiums or bidding eligibility
  • Bilingual support ensures Spanish-speaking supervisors can access the same information without translation delay

Projects

Projects — Organizing Safety by Jobsite
The Business Need

Construction companies manage multiple active projects simultaneously, each at a different physical location, each with different trades, different hazards, and sometimes different regulatory jurisdictions. A high-rise office building in downtown Chicago has completely different safety requirements than a residential subdivision in rural Texas. Safety management must be organized per project because inspections, incidents, workers, and documents all belong to specific jobsites.

Regulatory Landscape
🇺🇸United States
OSHA requires a Site-Specific Safety Plan (SSSP) for each project. Multi-employer worksites must identify the controlling employer, creating employer, exposing employer, and correcting employer. Each project must have an emergency action plan specific to that site (29 CFR 1926.35).
🇬🇧United Kingdom
CDM 2015 requires a Construction Phase Plan for every project where construction work is carried out. For projects with more than one contractor, a Principal Designer and Principal Contractor must be appointed. Notification to HSE is required if the project lasts more than 30 working days with more than 20 workers, or exceeds 500 person-days.
🇦🇺Australia
WHS Regulations require a WHS Management Plan for construction projects valued at $250,000 or more. The principal contractor must prepare and maintain this plan. Safe Work Method Statements (SWMS) are required for each high-risk activity within a project.
🇨🇦Canada
Provincial OHS regulations typically require a project-specific safety plan. In Alberta, a Prime Contractor designation is required for multi-employer sites. Ontario's Construction Projects regulation (O. Reg. 213/91) mandates a project-specific notice of project to the MOL for projects with a cost exceeding $50,000 or a duration exceeding three months.
Traditional Approach

Companies maintain manila folders or binders for each project, stored at the job trailer on-site. The safety director drives between sites to review documentation. Project information is duplicated across the safety plan, permit applications, and client submissions. When a worker moves between projects, their certifications must be manually verified at each site. Compliance tracking is done through periodic audits, often weeks or months apart.

How Kerf Helps

Project List: View all your projects with status filters (Active, Completed, On Hold). Each card shows the project name, address, and current compliance percentage.

New Project Form captures all the information needed for regulatory compliance in one place:

  • Project name and address
  • Client/owner name
  • Project type (Commercial, Residential, Industrial, Infrastructure, Renovation)
  • Estimated worker count and date range
  • 14 trade types (Electrical, Plumbing, Structural Steel, Concrete, Carpentry, HVAC, Roofing, Masonry, Painting, Demolition, Excavation, Drywall, Fire Protection, General Labor)
  • Special hazards description (e.g., "lead paint abatement," "confined space entry required")
  • Nearest hospital name and address (required by OSHA for emergency action plans)
  • Emergency contact name and phone

Project Detail is a comprehensive hub with a compliance ring showing your overall score, plus 9 tabs:

  1. Overview: Project summary, dates, trades, and hazard information
  2. Inspections: All inspections conducted at this project, filterable by type and date
  3. Toolbox Talks: Safety briefings delivered at this project, with attendance records
  4. Hazards: Identified hazards specific to this site
  5. Incidents: All incident reports filed at this project
  6. Workers: Workers currently assigned to this project, with their certification status
  7. Equipment: Equipment deployed to this project, with inspection status
  8. Documents: All safety documents (SSSP, JHAs, etc.) generated for this project
  9. Settings: Project-level configuration
Key Benefits
  • Single source of truth for all safety data per project, accessible from any device
  • Compliance percentage ring provides instant visibility into which projects need attention
  • Pre-structured form ensures no required regulatory information is accidentally omitted
  • Worker assignment tracking means you always know who is on which site and whether their certifications are current for the work being performed

Morning Safety Brief

Morning Safety Brief — Daily Site Risk Assessment
The Business Need

Every construction morning starts with a brief assessment: What are today's risks? Has anything changed since yesterday? Is the weather going to affect work at height? Are any new trades starting today that introduce different hazards? This "pre-task planning" process is how construction companies prevent the most common cause of incidents: unanticipated hazards.

Regulatory Landscape

US OSHA does not explicitly mandate a "morning brief," but pre-shift safety meetings are an industry best practice recognized in OSHA's Voluntary Protection Programs (VPP). Many GCs require daily safety huddles as a contract condition.

UK CDM 2015 requires site induction and ongoing communication about safety; daily briefings are a standard way to fulfill this.

AU WHS Code of Practice for Construction Work recommends daily pre-start meetings, especially when multiple trades are working simultaneously.

CA FLHA (Field Level Hazard Assessment) is a daily requirement on many Canadian construction sites, particularly in Alberta and British Columbia.

Traditional Approach

A site supervisor stands at the gate with a clipboard, discusses the day's work verbally, and notes any issues on a paper form. Weather checks are done by looking at the sky or a phone app. The form gets filed in the trailer and is rarely reviewed again unless an incident occurs and the company needs to demonstrate it was conducting daily safety briefings.

How Kerf Helps

The Morning Safety Brief page automatically generates a daily risk assessment for each project, including:

  • Risk Score (0-10): A calculated score based on current conditions: weather, active trades, open hazards, recent incidents, and certification alerts
  • Weather conditions: Current temperature, wind, and precipitation that may affect work at height, concrete pours, or outdoor welding
  • Alerts with action buttons: Specific warnings (e.g., "2 workers on site have certifications expiring this week") with one-click actions to resolve them
  • Recommended toolbox talk: Based on today's activities, Kerf suggests a relevant safety topic for the morning briefing
Key Benefits
  • Transforms a subjective daily ritual into a data-driven risk assessment
  • Catches conditions that humans might overlook (e.g., a certification that expired overnight)
  • Creates a documented record that the company conducted daily safety planning, which is valuable evidence in any enforcement action or litigation

Inspections

Inspections — Systematic Safety Verification
The Business Need

Construction sites change daily. New hazards appear as work progresses: an open excavation that was not there yesterday, scaffolding being erected, electrical systems being energized for the first time. Safety inspections are the mechanism by which someone physically walks the site, checks that safety controls are in place, and documents what they find. Without regular inspections, hazardous conditions go undetected until someone is injured.

Inspections also create a paper trail. If OSHA (or equivalent) visits and finds a violation, the company's defense often rests on proving they had a systematic inspection program and took corrective action when issues were found.

Regulatory Landscape
🇺🇸United States
Multiple OSHA standards require inspections at specific intervals:
• Scaffolds: "competent person" daily inspection before use (29 CFR 1926.451(f)(3))
• Excavations: daily inspection by competent person (29 CFR 1926.651(k))
• Fall protection: inspection of personal fall arrest systems before each use (29 CFR 1926.502)
• Electrical: assured equipment grounding conductor program requires inspection (29 CFR 1926.404(b)(1))
🇬🇧United Kingdom
The Work at Height Regulations 2005 require inspection of work platforms at specified intervals. CDM 2015 requires the principal contractor to ensure the construction phase plan includes arrangements for monitoring compliance. Scaffolding must be inspected before first use, after alteration, after any event likely to have affected stability, and at intervals not exceeding 7 days.
🇦🇺Australia
WHS Regulations specify inspection requirements for scaffolds (before first use and every 30 days), excavations, and temporary structures. Codes of Practice published by Safe Work Australia provide detailed guidance on inspection frequencies for each type of work.
🇨🇦Canada
Provincial regulations generally require regular workplace inspections. Ontario's Construction Projects regulation requires scaffold inspections by a competent worker before each shift. CSA Z797-18 covers workplace inspection processes. JHSC members have the right to conduct periodic inspections.
Traditional Approach

A competent person (a designation with specific meaning in OSHA regulations, referring to someone capable of identifying hazards and authorized to take corrective action) walks the site with a paper checklist. They mark items as satisfactory or deficient, note corrective actions needed, and file the form in the project binder. If a serious deficiency is found, they tell the foreman verbally. The paper form may or may not be reviewed by the safety director at month-end. Patterns across multiple inspections are nearly impossible to identify because the data is trapped on paper.

  1. Select the inspection type and the project it belongs to
  2. Enter the date, inspector name, weather conditions (temperature, wind speed, precipitation), and number of workers on site
  3. Work through the checklist, marking each item as Pass, Fail, or N/A (Not Applicable)

  • Inspection data feeds into the project compliance score on the dashboard
  • Records are instantly available if OSHA or other regulators request to see your inspection history during a compliance audit

A typical mid-size commercial project may require a 30-to-50-page Site-Specific Safety Plan, dozens of Job Hazard Analyses (one for each activity), toolbox talk scripts for weekly safety meetings, and various other documents. For companies managing 10+ projects, the documentation burden can overwhelm a safety department.

Workers & Certifications

Workers & Certifications — Credential Management
The Business Need

Construction workers must hold specific certifications to perform certain tasks. A forklift operator needs a forklift certification. A scaffold erector needs scaffold competent-person training. A worker entering a confined space needs confined-space entry training. These certifications expire (typically every 1-5 years) and must be renewed. If a worker operates equipment or performs a task without a valid certification, the employer faces significant legal liability and regulatory penalties. On a busy construction site with dozens or hundreds of workers, tracking who holds which certifications and when they expire is a major administrative challenge.

Regulatory Landscape

US OSHA requires specific training for virtually every hazardous activity. The OSHA 10-Hour and 30-Hour construction courses are foundational. Beyond that, specialized certifications are required: crane operators must hold NCCCO (National Commission for the Certification of Crane Operators) credentials per 29 CFR 1926 Subpart CC; forklift operators must be trained and evaluated per 29 CFR 1910.178; fall protection training is required under 29 CFR 1926.503.

UK CSCS (Construction Skills Certification Scheme) cards are the standard proof of competence. CDM 2015 requires that workers have the necessary skills, knowledge, and experience for the work they are doing. Specific certifications like IPAF (International Powered Access Federation) for aerial lifts and CPCS (Construction Plant Competence Scheme) for plant operators are widely required.

AU High-risk work licences are legally required under WHS Regulations for activities such as crane operation, scaffolding, rigging, and forklift operation. These are issued by state regulators and have specific renewal requirements. A White Card (General Construction Induction) is required before any person can enter a construction site.

CA Provincial requirements vary. Most provinces require an equivalent of basic construction safety training. Ontario requires a Working at Heights training certificate for workers on construction projects. Red Seal certifications apply to specific trades. CSA standards govern equipment operation training requirements.

Traditional Approach

Companies keep photocopies of worker certification cards in a filing cabinet at the office. A spreadsheet tracks expiration dates, but it is only updated when someone remembers. Before an OSHA inspection or GC audit, the safety director scrambles to verify that every worker on site has current certifications for the tasks they are performing. Workers who change projects must physically show their cards to a new site supervisor, who may or may not verify the expiration date.

  • Name, role, and trade
  • Language preference (English, Spanish, or Both) to ensure safety communications reach them in a language they understand
  • Employment status (Active, Inactive, Terminated)
  • Project assignments (which projects this worker is currently assigned to)

Toolbox Talks

Toolbox Talks — Bilingual Safety Briefings
The Business Need

A "toolbox talk" is a short (typically 10-15 minute) safety discussion conducted at the worksite before a shift begins or before a specific task. The name comes from the tradition of workers gathering around a toolbox to discuss safety. These talks reinforce safety awareness on topics relevant to the work being performed that day. They are one of the most effective ways to reduce incidents because they keep safety top-of-mind for workers. In the United States, the construction workforce is significantly bilingual, with an estimated 30% of construction workers being Spanish-speaking, making bilingual safety communication essential.

Regulatory Landscape

US OSHA requires that safety training be delivered "in a language and vocabulary the employee can understand" (multiple standards). Toolbox talks are an accepted method of ongoing training. Many GCs contractually require weekly or daily toolbox talks from their subcontractors, with documented attendance.

UK CDM 2015 requires that workers are provided with information and instruction regarding health and safety. Toolbox talks are a widely accepted delivery mechanism. HSE publishes guidance on effective communication for construction sites.

AU WHS Act requires workers to be informed and trained about safety matters. Pre-start meetings and toolbox talks are standard practice and referenced in multiple Codes of Practice.

CA Provincial regulations require ongoing worker education. Alberta OHS requires regular safety meetings, and toolbox talks/tailgate meetings are the standard method of compliance.

Traditional Approach

A supervisor downloads a generic toolbox talk PDF from the internet, reads it aloud to workers, and passes around a sign-in sheet on a clipboard. The talk may not be relevant to the specific work being performed that day. For Spanish-speaking workers, the content is either not translated (meaning they do not actually understand it) or poorly machine-translated. The sign-in sheet is filed and forgotten. There is no way to verify that the talk was actually delivered as intended or that workers understood the content.

How Kerf Helps

Creating a Toolbox Talk:

  1. Select a topic (from a library of common construction safety topics or enter a custom topic)
  2. Choose the target audience (specific trades, all workers, supervisors)
  3. Set the expected duration
  4. Add any custom talking points specific to your project
  5. Kerf generates a bilingual (English/Spanish) safety briefing using AI, customized to your project context

Delivering a Toolbox Talk:

  • Delivery mode presents the talk content in a readable format for the presenter
  • Digital signatures replace paper sign-in sheets. Each attending worker signs digitally on the device
  • Attendance tracking records exactly who attended, with timestamps, creating an auditable record
Key Benefits
  • AI-generated content is relevant to the actual work and hazards at the project, not generic
  • Bilingual generation ensures Spanish-speaking workers receive the same information, meeting OSHA's language requirement
  • Digital attendance tracking with timestamps creates stronger evidence of compliance than paper sign-in sheets
  • Talks can be linked to projects and included in the project's documentation package for GC or client submissions

Incident Management

Incident Management — From Report to Resolution
The Business Need

When a worker is injured, a near-miss occurs, or property is damaged, the company must document what happened, investigate the root cause, implement corrective actions, and report the incident to the appropriate regulatory agency (if it meets reporting thresholds). The quality and speed of incident management directly affects worker safety, insurance costs, and legal exposure. Poor incident management leads to repeat incidents, higher workers' compensation premiums, and regulatory penalties.

Regulatory Landscape
🇺🇸United States
Recordkeeping (29 CFR 1904): Employers with 10+ employees must maintain OSHA 300 Log for all recordable injuries/illnesses. A "recordable" injury is one requiring medical treatment beyond first aid.
Reporting: Fatalities must be reported within 8 hours. In-patient hospitalizations, amputations, and loss of an eye must be reported within 24 hours (29 CFR 1904.39).
Penalty: Failure to report a fatality within 8 hours can result in penalties up to $161,323.
🇬🇧United Kingdom
RIDDOR: Certain workplace incidents must be reported to HSE: deaths, specified injuries (fractures, amputations, etc.), over-7-day incapacitations, occupational diseases, and dangerous occurrences. Reports must be filed within 10 days (or 15 days for over-7-day injuries). Online reporting via the HSE website is the standard method.
🇦🇺Australia
Notifiable Incidents: WHS Act 2011 defines three categories that must be reported to the state regulator immediately by phone: death, serious injury or illness, and dangerous incidents. The site must be preserved until an inspector arrives or directs otherwise. Written notification must follow within 48 hours.
🇨🇦Canada
Provincial reporting: Each province defines reportable incidents differently. Generally, fatalities and critical injuries must be reported immediately. Ontario requires written notice to the MOL within 48 hours for critical injuries. WSIB or WorkSafeBC claims must be filed within specified periods for workers' compensation purposes.
Traditional Approach

A supervisor fills out a paper incident report form, which makes its way to the safety director's desk within a day or two. The safety director determines whether the incident is OSHA-recordable by consulting a flowchart, then manually adds it to the paper OSHA 300 Log. Root cause investigation is informal. Corrective actions are verbal agreements that may or may not be followed up. Near-misses are rarely reported because the paperwork is seen as burdensome and there is no positive incentive for workers to report.

How Kerf Helps

Incident Report Form captures all essential information:

  • Date, time, and exact location of the incident
  • Severity level: Fatality, Hospitalization, Medical Treatment, First Aid, Near Miss, or Property Damage
  • Detailed description of what happened
  • Persons involved and witnesses
  • Immediate actions taken

Auto-classification is a significant time-saver. Based on the severity and details you enter, Kerf automatically determines:

  • Whether the incident is OSHA recordable (must be entered on the 300 Log)
  • Whether the incident is OSHA reportable (must be called in to OSHA within 8 or 24 hours)

Workflow tracking moves each incident through four stages:

  1. Reported: Initial report filed
  2. Investigating: Root cause analysis underway
  3. Corrective Actions: Fixes identified and being implemented
  4. Closed: Investigation complete, corrective actions verified
Critical timing reminder In the US, a fatality must be reported to OSHA within 8 hours. A hospitalization, amputation, or loss of an eye must be reported within 24 hours. Kerf's auto-classification flags reportable events immediately so you do not miss these deadlines.
Key Benefits
  • Auto-classification eliminates the risk of misclassifying an incident, which is one of the most common OSHA citation categories
  • Structured workflow ensures incidents are investigated and closed, not forgotten
  • Near-miss reporting is as easy as reporting an actual injury, encouraging a safety culture where hazards are reported before they cause harm
  • All incident data feeds into TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate) calculations on the Analytics page

Mock OSHA Inspection

Mock OSHA Inspection — Simulated Compliance Audit
The Business Need

An OSHA compliance officer can arrive at any construction site without advance notice. When they do, they will review your safety documentation, training records, inspection logs, and incident history. They will walk the site looking for hazards. Any violations they find result in citations carrying financial penalties and a public record. Companies that are well-prepared for OSHA inspections have dramatically fewer citations and lower penalties because many issues can be corrected before they become violations. A "mock inspection" is the practice of conducting an internal audit that simulates what OSHA would look for, so the company can identify and fix gaps before the real inspector arrives.

Regulatory Landscape

US OSHA's penalty structure makes preparation financially critical. As of 2024, a single serious violation can cost up to $16,131. A willful or repeat violation can reach $161,323. OSHA also considers "good faith" efforts (including systematic self-auditing) as a penalty reduction factor of up to 25%. Having a documented mock inspection program demonstrates good faith.

UK HSE inspectors follow a similar approach. Demonstrating a proactive safety management system, including internal audits, is recognized under the Enforcement Management Model (EMM) as a mitigating factor.

AU State regulators consider an organization's safety management system when determining enforcement action. Internal auditing is a recognized component of an effective WHS management system.

CA COR (Certificate of Recognition) programs in provinces like Alberta explicitly require internal audits as part of maintaining certification, which qualifies companies for workers' compensation premium discounts.

Traditional Approach

Companies hire external safety consultants to conduct mock inspections, typically at $2,000 to $5,000+ per visit. The consultant walks the site, reviews documentation, and produces a report. This happens once or twice a year, leaving months-long gaps where issues can develop undetected. Some companies have internal safety staff perform mock audits, but these are limited by the staff's knowledge and are often less rigorous than what an actual OSHA inspector would examine.

  • Penalty estimates: Approximate penalty amounts based on current OSHA penalty schedules

A score of 85+ (grade A or B) indicates strong compliance. Most companies that maintain a Kerf readiness score of 85 or above find that actual OSHA inspections result in minimal or no citations.

Equipment Tracking

Equipment — Fleet and Inspection Management
The Business Need

Construction companies operate expensive, potentially dangerous equipment: cranes, forklifts, aerial lifts (boom lifts, scissor lifts), vehicles, and scaffolding systems. This equipment must be regularly inspected, properly maintained, and operated only by certified personnel. Equipment failures are a leading cause of construction fatalities. Beyond safety, equipment represents millions of dollars in assets that must be tracked, maintained, and assigned to projects efficiently.

Regulatory Landscape

US OSHA requires pre-operation inspections for most powered equipment (29 CFR 1926 Subpart O for motor vehicles, Subpart CC for cranes, Subpart L for scaffolds). DOT (Department of Transportation) compliance is required for vehicles used on public roads. Annual crane inspections by a qualified inspector are mandatory.

UK PUWER (Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998) requires that all work equipment is maintained in an efficient state. LOLER (Lifting Operations and Lifting Equipment Regulations 1998) requires regular examination of lifting equipment by a competent person.

AU WHS Regulations require regular inspection of plant (the Australian term for equipment) used on construction sites. Registered plant such as cranes requires periodic inspection by a competent person. Design registration is required for certain plant categories.

CA Provincial regulations require pre-use inspections of equipment. CSA standards (e.g., CSA Z150 for mobile cranes) define inspection and maintenance requirements. Annual crane inspections are mandated in most provinces.

Traditional Approach

Equipment inspection records are kept in binders at the equipment yard or in the operator's cab. Knowing which equipment is at which project requires phone calls. Maintenance schedules are tracked in spreadsheets or whiteboards. When an inspection is overdue, nobody notices until the equipment has already been used in a non-compliant state.

OSHA Log (300/300A)

OSHA Log — Injury and Illness Recordkeeping
The Business Need

In the United States, most employers with 10 or more employees must maintain a log of all work-related injuries and illnesses that meet the recording criteria. This log (OSHA Form 300) and its annual summary (Form 300A) are the primary instruments by which injury rates are calculated and compared across the industry. These rates directly affect a company's EMR (Experience Modification Rate), which in turn determines workers' compensation insurance premiums. A high injury rate can increase premiums by 50% or more and disqualify a company from bidding on many projects.

Regulatory Landscape

US 29 CFR 1904 governs recordkeeping. Key requirements:

  • OSHA Form 300 must be maintained for each establishment (typically each fixed workplace or project office) throughout the year
  • OSHA Form 300A (annual summary) must be posted in a visible location from February 1 through April 30 of the following year
  • Records must be retained for 5 years following the end of the calendar year they cover
  • Certain industries and establishments with 250+ employees must electronically submit injury/illness data to OSHA annually

UK Equivalent recordkeeping is handled through RIDDOR reporting and internal accident books (required under the Social Security (Claims and Payments) Regulations 1979).

AU WHS Regulations require employers to keep records of notifiable incidents and maintain a register of injuries.

CA Provincial regulations require maintenance of workplace injury/illness records. WSIB (Ontario) and equivalent provincial boards require reporting for workers' compensation purposes.

300A Posting Requirement The OSHA 300A annual summary must be posted in a conspicuous place where employee notices are customarily posted, from February 1 through April 30 each year. Kerf displays a posting reminder during this period to help you stay compliant.
Traditional Approach

Companies maintain the OSHA 300 Log as a paper form or Excel spreadsheet. Each recordable injury is manually entered with details about the employee, injury type, and days away from work. At year-end, someone manually tallies the numbers to produce the 300A summary and calculate incidence rates. Errors in classification (recording a first-aid case as recordable, or failing to record a recordable case) are common and are frequently cited by OSHA.

  • OSHA Form 300: A digital log of work-related injuries and illnesses. Entries can be added manually or auto-populated from incident reports that Kerf has classified as OSHA-recordable
  • OSHA Form 300A: Annual summary is automatically calculated from the 300 Log data. Total hours worked, number of cases, days away from work, and incidence rates are computed for you
  • Incidence rate calculations: TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate), DART (Days Away, Restricted, or Transferred) rate, and other metrics are automatically calculated using the standard formula: (Number of cases x 200,000) / Total hours worked
  • Year selector: View logs and summaries for any year. Records are retained digitally and can be accessed at any time (OSHA requires 5-year retention)
  • Add/delete entries: Full CRUD (create, read, update, delete) capability for log entries
  • Posting period warning: A prominent banner appears between February 1 and April 30 reminding you to print and post the 300A summary

  • Automatic incidence rate calculations eliminate math errors that can misrepresent your safety performance
  • Integration with incident reports reduces duplicate data entry and classification errors
  • Digital retention ensures records are available for the required 5-year period and beyond
  • Posting period reminders prevent a common compliance oversight

Regional Compliance

Regional Compliance — Jurisdiction-Specific Requirements
The Business Need

Safety regulations are not uniform even within a single country. In the United States, 22 states operate their own OSHA-approved State Plans, which can impose requirements that are stricter than federal OSHA. A company operating in California faces different (and more stringent) requirements than one in Texas. Similarly, Australian states have variations on the harmonised WHS law, and Canadian provinces each have their own OHS legislation. Companies working across multiple states or regions must understand and comply with whichever jurisdiction's rules apply at each worksite.

Regulatory Landscape

US Examples of state-specific requirements that go beyond federal OSHA:

  • California (Cal/OSHA): Heat Illness Prevention standard, Aerosol Transmissible Diseases standard, Injury and Illness Prevention Program (IIPP) required for all employers
  • Washington (WA L&I): Safety committee requirements differ from federal, agricultural safety rules are more extensive
  • New York: Scaffold Safety Act (Labor Law 240) creates strict liability for gravity-related injuries, among the most plaintiff-friendly laws in the country

UK While mostly uniform, Scotland has some separate health and safety legislation. Northern Ireland has its own HSE equivalent (HSENI).

AU Victoria and Western Australia have not fully adopted the harmonised WHS Act. Victoria operates under the Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004, and WA under the Work Health and Safety Act 2020 (adopted late with some variations).

CA Every province has its own OHS legislation, enforcement agency, and specific requirements. Alberta's OHS Act, Ontario's OHSA, and British Columbia's Workers Compensation Act each have unique provisions.

Traditional Approach

Companies that work in multiple jurisdictions either hire local safety consultants in each region or rely on their safety director to research and understand the differences. This often results in a "highest common denominator" approach where the company applies the strictest rules everywhere, or (worse) applies only federal rules and unknowingly violates state-specific requirements.

How Kerf Helps
  • Dropdown selector: Choose your state/region and Kerf displays the specific requirements that apply beyond the base federal or national standards
  • Multi-jurisdiction support: View requirements for US states, UK regions, Australian states, and Canadian provinces
  • Gap identification: See what additional compliance steps are needed when you start a project in a new jurisdiction
Key Benefits
  • Prevents the common mistake of applying only federal standards in a State Plan state
  • Enables companies to expand into new geographic areas with confidence
  • Reduces dependence on expensive local consultants for basic compliance information

Prequalification

Prequalification — Contractor Vetting Platforms
The Business Need

"Prequalification" is the process by which a general contractor or project owner verifies that a subcontractor meets minimum safety standards before allowing them to work on a project. Third-party platforms like ISNetworld, Avetta, and BROWZ serve as intermediaries: the subcontractor uploads safety documents, training records, insurance certificates, and answers questionnaires. The platform evaluates the submissions and assigns a compliance grade. General contractors then use this grade to decide which subs are qualified to bid on their projects. Being fully prequalified on these platforms is essentially a prerequisite for winning large commercial or industrial construction projects.

Regulatory Landscape

Prequalification itself is not a legal requirement, but it exists because of legal requirements:

US The multi-employer doctrine means GCs can be cited by OSHA for subcontractor safety failures. This creates a powerful business incentive for GCs to verify that their subs have strong safety programs before they arrive on site. Insurance carriers also require GCs to vet subcontractor safety records to maintain coverage.

UK CDM 2015 requires the client and principal contractor to ensure that contractors they appoint are competent. Prequalification questionnaires (PQQs) are the standard mechanism for demonstrating competence.

AU Many state government and large private clients require subcontractors to meet prequalification standards. Federal government construction projects require compliance with the WHS Accreditation Scheme.

CA COR (Certificate of Recognition) programs and ISNetworld are widely used by oil and gas and large construction clients in Alberta and BC to prequalify contractors.

Traditional Approach

The safety director spends days or weeks gathering documents from various departments (insurance certificates from accounting, training records from HR, OSHA logs from safety, company information from admin), then manually uploads each document to ISNetworld, Avetta, or BROWZ. The questionnaire sections require answering hundreds of questions about the company's safety programs, policies, and history. This process is repeated (often differently) for each platform. When documents expire or are updated, the safety director must remember to upload the new versions.

The Business Need

On a typical construction project, a General Contractor (GC) manages multiple subcontractors (subs). Environmental Programs

Environmental — Hazardous Substance & Environmental Compliance
The Business Need

Construction activities frequently involve exposure to hazardous substances (silica dust from concrete cutting, lead paint in renovation, asbestos in demolition) and environmental impacts (stormwater runoff from disturbed soil, noise affecting neighboring communities, hazardous material handling). These require specific management programs with monitoring, training, and documentation. Unlike general safety, environmental compliance often involves both worker protection regulations (OSHA) and environmental protection regulations (EPA or equivalent), creating a dual compliance requirement.

Regulatory Landscape

US Key regulations include:

  • Silica: 29 CFR 1926.1153 (Respirable Crystalline Silica in Construction). Exposure limit of 50 micrograms per cubic meter as an 8-hour TWA (time-weighted average). Requires a written exposure control plan, exposure monitoring, medical surveillance, and a designated competent person.
  • Lead: 29 CFR 1926.62 (Lead in Construction). Action level of 30 micrograms per cubic meter. Requires exposure monitoring, medical surveillance, and protective measures.
  • Asbestos: 29 CFR 1926.1101 (Asbestos in Construction). Requires identification before demolition/renovation, specific work practices, and licensed abatement contractors.
  • Stormwater: EPA Clean Water Act requires a SWPPP (Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan) for construction sites disturbing 1+ acre of land.
  • Noise: 29 CFR 1926.52 (Occupational Noise Exposure). Action level of 85 dBA as an 8-hour TWA.
  • HazMat: 29 CFR 1926.65 (Hazardous Waste Operations and Emergency Response - HAZWOPER).

UK COSHH (Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002) provides the framework for chemical exposure management. The Control of Asbestos Regulations 2012, Control of Lead at Work Regulations 2002, and Environmental Protection Act 1990 cover the equivalent areas.

AU WHS Regulations include specific chapters for hazardous chemicals, asbestos management, noise, and lead. State EPA regulations cover environmental impacts including stormwater management.

CA Provincial OHS regulations cover workplace chemical exposures. Environmental protection falls under provincial environmental acts. Asbestos regulations vary by province.

Traditional Approach

Environmental programs are often the weakest area in a construction company's safety management because they require specialized knowledge (industrial hygiene, environmental engineering) that most safety directors do not have. Companies either hire external industrial hygienists for monitoring or simply neglect exposure monitoring until an incident or complaint forces action. Program documentation is scattered across multiple binders and is rarely updated.

  • Program status: Whether the program is active, its compliance status, and any pending actions
  • Exposure monitoring records: Log monitoring results with dates, locations, measured levels, and comparison to regulatory limits
  • Regulatory references: Direct references to the applicable CFR sections (e.g., 29 CFR 1926.1153 for silica) so your team knows exactly which standard applies

  • Monitoring records create auditable evidence of compliance for both OSHA and EPA inspectors

Analytics & Rates

Analytics — Safety Performance Metrics
The Business Need

Construction companies are judged by their safety metrics. Clients, GCs, and insurance companies all evaluate contractors based on quantifiable safety performance. The two most important metrics are TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate) and EMR (Experience Modification Rate). A TRIR above the industry average can disqualify a company from bidding on projects. An EMR above 1.0 means the company pays higher workers' compensation premiums than the industry average, directly impacting profitability. Understanding these numbers and how to improve them is essential for business survival.

Regulatory Landscape

US OSHA requires calculation of incidence rates using the formula: (Number of cases x 200,000) / Total hours worked. The 200,000 figure represents the hours 100 full-time employees would work in a year. This standardization allows comparison across companies of different sizes. The Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes industry average rates annually.

UK HSE publishes accident rates per 100,000 workers. The HSE's Annual Statistics of Workplace Health and Safety provide industry benchmarks.

AU Safe Work Australia publishes national and state-level statistics on workplace injuries and fatalities. Incidence rates are calculated per 1,000 workers or per million hours worked.

CA Provincial workers' compensation boards publish industry injury rate statistics. WSIB (Ontario) uses a lost-time injury rate. WorkSafeBC publishes injury rates by industry classification.

Traditional Approach

At year-end, the safety director manually calculates TRIR and DART from the OSHA 300 Log using a calculator or spreadsheet. The EMR is received from the workers' compensation insurance carrier and is accepted without verification. There is no easy way to see trends over time or to compare the company's rates to industry averages. Strategic decisions about safety investments are made based on gut feeling rather than data.

Understanding EMR The Experience Modification Rate (EMR) compares your actual losses to the expected losses for a company of your size in your industry. An EMR of 1.0 is average. Below 1.0 means you are safer than average (and pay lower premiums). Above 1.0 means you are worse than average (and pay higher premiums). Glossary

Key terms used throughout this manual and in the construction safety industry:

29 CFR 1926
Title 29, Code of Federal Regulations, Part 1926. The primary US federal regulation governing safety in the construction industry. Organized into subparts covering specific hazard categories (e.g., Subpart M for fall protection, Subpart CC for cranes).
CDM 2015
Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015. The UK's primary regulation for managing health and safety on construction projects. Assigns duties to clients, principal designers, principal contractors, contractors, and designers.
Competent Person
A term with specific legal meaning in OSHA regulations. A person who is capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards in the surroundings or working conditions which are unsanitary, hazardous, or dangerous to employees, and who has authorization to take prompt corrective measures to eliminate them. Many OSHA standards require a "competent person" for specific tasks like scaffold inspection, excavation evaluation, and fall protection planning.
COR (Certificate of Recognition)
A Canadian program (primarily in western provinces) that certifies employers who have implemented health and safety management systems meeting established standards. COR-certified employers typically qualify for workers' compensation premium discounts.
COSHH
Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (UK). Requires employers to assess and control exposure to hazardous substances in the workplace, including chemicals, fumes, dusts, and biological agents.
DART Rate
Days Away, Restricted, or Transferred rate. A more specific injury metric than TRIR that counts only cases resulting in days away from work, restricted work activity, or job transfer. Formula: (DART cases x 200,000) / Total hours worked.
DOT
Department of Transportation (US). Governs the operation of commercial motor vehicles on public roads, including inspection requirements, driver qualifications, and hours-of-service rules.
EMR (Experience Modification Rate)
A multiplier applied to a company's workers' compensation insurance premium. Calculated by the National Council on Compensation Insurance (NCCI) or state equivalent based on 3 years of loss history compared to industry expected losses. EMR of 1.0 is average; below 1.0 means better-than-average safety performance; above 1.0 means worse. Directly impacts insurance costs and bidding eligibility.
Fatal Four
The four most common causes of construction worker deaths in the United States, as identified by OSHA: Falls (38.7%), Struck-By Object (9.4%), Electrocution (7.2%), and Caught-In/Between (5.4%). Together, these account for more than 60% of construction fatalities annually.
FLHA (Field Level Hazard Assessment)
A daily hazard assessment process widely used in Canadian construction (especially Alberta and BC). Workers assess hazards at their specific work location before starting work each day. Similar in purpose to a JHA but focused on real-time field conditions.
GC (General Contractor)
The company that has the primary contract with the project owner and manages the overall construction project. The GC hires and coordinates subcontractors and is generally the "controlling employer" for OSHA purposes, meaning they have overall responsibility for site safety.
GFCI
Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter. An electrical safety device that disconnects a circuit when it detects an imbalance between the outgoing and incoming current, protecting against electric shock. Required on all construction sites for 120-volt, single-phase, 15- and 20-ampere receptacle outlets (29 CFR 1926.404(b)(1)).
HAZWOPER
Hazardous Waste Operations and Emergency Response (29 CFR 1910.120 / 29 CFR 1926.65). OSHA standard governing worker safety at hazardous waste sites, during emergency response to hazardous substance releases, and at treatment, storage, and disposal facilities.
HSE
Health and Safety Executive (UK). The national regulator for workplace health and safety in Great Britain. Enforces the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and related regulations. Can issue improvement notices, prohibition notices, and prosecute employers for safety failures.
JHA (Job Hazard Analysis)
A systematic process of identifying hazards associated with each step of a job and determining the best way to control those hazards. Also called JSA (Job Safety Analysis). In the UK, the equivalent is a RAMS (Risk Assessment and Method Statement). In Australia, the equivalent for high-risk construction work is a SWMS (Safe Work Method Statement).
JHSC (Joint Health and Safety Committee)
A Canadian requirement for workplaces over a certain size (varies by province). A committee composed of both employer and worker representatives that meets regularly to address health and safety concerns. Similar to a safety committee but with specific legal requirements for composition and function.
Multi-Employer Doctrine
An OSHA enforcement policy that allows citations to be issued to employers who did not directly create a hazard. Four employer types are recognized: Creating (created the hazard), Exposing (workers are exposed), Correcting (responsible for correcting), and Controlling (has general supervisory authority). This doctrine is why GCs can be cited for subcontractor safety failures.
NCCCO
National Commission for the Certification of Crane Operators. The leading US certification body for crane operators. OSHA requires crane operators to be certified by an accredited testing organization, and NCCCO is the most widely recognized.
OSHA
Occupational Safety and Health Administration (US). A federal agency under the Department of Labor that sets and enforces workplace safety standards. Created by the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970. Conducts workplace inspections and can issue citations with monetary penalties.
OSHA 300 Log
OSHA Form 300, the Log of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses. A running log maintained throughout the year recording each recordable work-related injury or illness, including the employee's name, job title, date, description, and classification of the case.
OSHA 300A
OSHA Form 300A, the Summary of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses. An annual summary that tallies the data from the 300 Log. Must be certified by a company executive and posted in a visible location from February 1 through April 30 each year.
PCBU
Person Conducting a Business or Undertaking. An Australian WHS Act term that broadens the duty holder beyond just "employers" to include anyone who directs or influences work, including principal contractors, self-employed persons, and companies that engage workers through labor hire arrangements.
PPE
Personal Protective Equipment. The last line of defense in the hierarchy of controls. Includes hard hats, safety glasses, high-visibility vests, steel-toed boots, gloves, hearing protection, and respiratory protection. OSHA requires employers to provide PPE at no cost to employees (29 CFR 1926.95).
RAMS
Risk Assessment and Method Statement (UK). A combined document that identifies hazards, assesses risks, and describes the method of work including safety controls. Required for virtually all construction activities in the UK under CDM 2015. Equivalent to a JHA/SWMS in other jurisdictions.
RIDDOR
Reporting of Injuries, Diseases and Dangerous Occurrences Regulations 2013 (UK). Requires employers to report certain workplace incidents to HSE, including deaths, specified injuries, over-7-day incapacitations, certain occupational diseases, and dangerous occurrences.
SSSP
Site-Specific Safety Plan. A comprehensive safety document tailored to a specific construction project. Covers the project scope, identified hazards, safety procedures, emergency plans, training requirements, and roles and responsibilities. While not explicitly mandated by a single OSHA standard, it is required by most GCs and is a key component of OSHA's VPP (Voluntary Protection Programs).
State Plan State
A US state that has an OSHA-approved State Plan, meaning it operates its own occupational safety and health program instead of (or in addition to) federal OSHA. State Plan standards must be at least as effective as federal OSHA standards but can be more stringent. Currently 22 states plus several territories operate State Plans.
SWMS
Safe Work Method Statement (Australia). A legally required document for high-risk construction work under WHS Regulations. Identifies the high-risk work, describes the hazards, and sets out the measures to control risks. Must be prepared before high-risk work begins. Roughly equivalent to a JHA (US) or RAMS (UK) but with specific legal requirements.
SWPPP
Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan. A plan required under the US Clean Water Act (administered by EPA) for construction sites that disturb one or more acres of land. Describes Best Management Practices (BMPs) for preventing stormwater runoff from carrying sediment and pollutants into waterways.
TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate)
The most widely used safety performance metric in US construction. Calculated as: (Total recordable cases x 200,000) / Total hours worked. The 200,000 represents the approximate hours 100 full-time employees work in a year. The 2023 industry average for construction is approximately 2.8. A lower TRIR indicates better safety performance.
TWA (Time-Weighted Average)
The average concentration of a substance (chemical, dust, noise) over a specified time period, typically 8 hours. Used to compare workplace exposures to regulatory limits. For example, the OSHA permissible exposure limit for respirable crystalline silica is 50 micrograms per cubic meter as an 8-hour TWA.
VPP (Voluntary Protection Programs)
An OSHA program that recognizes employers and workers who have implemented effective safety and health management systems and maintain injury/illness rates below national averages. VPP sites are generally exempt from routine OSHA inspections. Participation is voluntary and requires a rigorous application and on-site evaluation.
WHS Act 2011
Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Australia). The model legislation developed by Safe Work Australia to harmonise OHS laws across Australian states and territories. Adopted by most jurisdictions, with Victoria and Western Australia having their own versions. Establishes duties for PCBUs, officers, and workers.
WSIB
Workplace Safety and Insurance Board (Ontario, Canada). The provincial agency that administers the workplace injury insurance system, providing benefits to workers injured on the job and collecting premiums from employers. Equivalent agencies exist in other provinces (WorkSafeBC in British Columbia, WCB in Alberta, etc.).
