Naming Analysis

PRE-LAUNCH • ZERO BRAND EQUITY
Context
Starting Point
This app has not launched. There are no customers, no brand equity, no SEO to protect. The name choice has zero switching cost. We're choosing the launch name, not rebranding.

Kerf is a construction operations platform: safety inspections, incident reporting, hazard reports, toolbox talks, OSHA compliance, environmental monitoring, equipment management, worker management, project management, voice transcription, AI document generation. The product architecture is already broader than "safety" — it's a full construction ops platform that started with safety as the entry point.

The question: should the launch name be Kerf, or something that matches the actual scope of the product?

The Most Important Perspective
How Construction Workers Actually Think About Software

What the research found

91% of B2B construction purchases are influenced by word-of-mouth. The primary discovery channel is a superintendent or PM telling another one "you need this." The name needs to survive being yelled across a jobsite, texted with gloves on, and recommended in a trailer over coffee.

What workers respect in a name

Directness
No BS, no fluff

Say what you mean. Don't try to sound smarter than the user.

Durability
Sounds like it can take a hit

The name should feel physical, not corporate.

Brevity
Two syllables max

They have 30 seconds between tasks. Long names get shortened or replaced with "the app."

Realness
Exists in the physical world

Should sound like something on a jobsite, not in a pitch deck.

What workers would mock

  • Anything ending in "-ify," "-ly," "-io"
  • Anything with "smart," "AI," "360," "Hub," "Cloud," or "Suite" in it
  • Cutesy misspellings (Buildr, Safetyy)
  • Anything that needs a TED talk to explain
  • Anything that sounds like "another way to get the field guys to do office work"
"This app development is such an office move. It's a nightmare for field workers. It's another way to get the field guys to do most of the office work. Taking away focus from actual field work."
— Construction worker review, Capterra

The tool brand playbook, not the SaaS playbook

Construction workers are loyal to tool brands: DeWalt, Milwaukee, Hilti, Makita, Bosch, Klein, STIHL. These names share patterns that SaaS names don't follow.

Tool Brand PatternSaaS Pattern (avoid)
Short: 1-2 syllables (Bosch, Klein, Hilti, DeWalt)Compound descriptors: PlanGrid, BuildOps, FieldWire
Proper-noun feeling: sounds like a name, not a descriptionFunction-in-name: SafetyCulture, TimeKeeper, ProjectPay
Consonant-heavy, punchy sounds (K, T, B, D)Soft/generic sounds (-flow, -sync, -hub)
No function described: STIHL doesn't say "chainsaw"Category declared: Buildertrend says "builders only"
Earned meaning through quality, not marketingTries to sound big before earning it
The insight: Software names that follow tool brand patterns (short, physical, consonant-heavy, no function described) will feel native to construction workers. Names that follow SaaS patterns will trigger the "office move" reflex.

The jobsite test

Every candidate name must pass this test: "Pull it up on [Name]" said by a foreman on a concrete pour with a pump truck running. If the listener can't parse it, the name fails. Hard plosive consonants (B, D, G, K, P, T) cut through diesel generators and impact drivers. Soft fricatives (S, F, H) and sibilants get swallowed.

Names that work on a jobsite: Procore (hard P, K), Raken (hard R, K), Bluebeam (hard B, B). Names that struggle: Kerf (soft S, soft F), SafetyCulture (5 syllables).

What Actually Works
Names That Survived Conflict Checks

After eliminating everything with existing conflicts, four candidates remain clean. We've evaluated each through the construction worker lens.

Recommended — Construction-native
Gangbox
2 syllables • Construction term
The gang box is the big metal toolbox on every construction site. Every single person on a jobsite knows what it is — it's where you keep your tools, your safety equipment, your gear. It's communal (the whole crew accesses it), tough (heavy steel that sits outside in all weather), and essential. The metaphor is perfect: your digital gang box for the whole crew's tools.

Worker test: "Check the gangbox" is already something foremen say 10 times a day. The name integrates into jobsite language seamlessly. "Log it in Gangbox" — natural. "Pull it up on Gangbox" — clean.

Phonetics: Hard G + hard B + hard K/X. Three plosives in one word. Cuts through any jobsite noise. Two syllables.

Expansion: A gang box holds everything — tools, PPE, documents, plans. The name naturally extends to any construction ops feature without explanation.

Domain: gangbox.com is registered but not serving — likely parked/acquirable. No software competitors. A physical tools company uses "thegangbox.com."

Jobsite: A+ Worker resonance: A+ Expansion: 5/5 Conflicts: None
Kerf
1 syllable • Construction term
The width of a saw cut — the precise gap a blade makes through material. Every carpenter, metalworker, and millworker knows this word. It implies precision, cutting through with purpose, and getting the measurement exactly right. One syllable, hard K, feels like a tool brand.

Worker test: "Log it in Kerf" — fast, clean, one syllable. Feels like something you'd find next to your DeWalt. "Pull it up on Kerf" — sharp and memorable.

Phonetics: Hard K attack, RF ending. One syllable. Maximum noise penetration. Sounds like STIHL, Klein, Bosch — the tool brand pattern.

Expansion: Abstract enough to hold any meaning. Like "Procore" — the word becomes the brand.

Domain: kerf.build redirects to GoDaddy "for sale." Prior software startup (time-series DB) is dead. Domain is acquirable.

Risk: Not every trade uses the word "kerf" daily. Ironworkers and heavy civil crews may not connect immediately. But tool brands earn their meaning — Hilti didn't mean anything until it did.

Jobsite: A+ Worker resonance: A Expansion: 5/5 Conflicts: Clear
Worth Considering — Coined / abstract
Bildek
2 syllables • Coined word
Coined from "build" + Germanic/Old English roots. Feels like a tool brand name — similar phonetic shape to DeWalt or Hilti. Three hard plosives (B, D, K). The "bild" root connects to building across Germanic languages (German: Bild = image/picture, bilden = to form/create).

Worker test: "Log it in Bildek" — works. Feels industrial, not corporate. Sounds like something that exists in a Hilti catalogue.

Phonetics: BIL-dek. Hard B, D, K. Two syllables. Excellent noise penetration.

Expansion: Coined words have unlimited expansion (Procore model). No pre-existing meaning to fight.

Domain: bildek.com registered but not active (self-signed cert). Likely parked, acquirable. Minor Latvian entity exists (unrelated).

Jobsite: A Worker resonance: B+ Expansion: 5/5 Conflicts: Clear
Daktik
2 syllables • Coined word
Coined from Greek "daktikos" (relating to fingers/touch) + "taktik" (tactical). Four hard plosives (D, K, T, K). Sounds sharp, precise, modern. The cleanest namespace of any candidate — no company or product uses this word anywhere.

Worker test: "Log it in Daktik" — clean but slightly foreign-sounding. Doesn't immediately feel like construction, more like a tech brand.

Phonetics: DAK-tik. Aggressive consonant pattern. Cuts through anything. But the double K sound could feel clinical rather than rugged.

Domain: daktik.com appears unregistered or minimally parked. Strongest domain availability of any candidate.

Jobsite: A Worker resonance: B Expansion: 5/5 Conflicts: Clear
Fallback — Lowest switching cost
SiteForge
2 syllables • Compound
Keeps the "Forge" identity while dropping "Safety." The original SiteForge software company (workflow orchestration, Herndon VA) appears to be dead. The .com may be acquirable. No construction tech conflict exists.

Worker test: "Log it in SiteForge" — functional but doesn't excite. Still has the F fricative problem. Still carries the forgery connotation.

Trade-off: Easiest name change from Kerf. But it inherits most of Kerf's phonetic weaknesses and the "forge = fake" risk.

Jobsite: B+ Worker resonance: B Expansion: 5/5 Conflicts: Low (dead company)

Decision
Recommendation

Launch as Gangbox.

It's the only name that passes every test simultaneously: construction workers already know the word, it's two syllables with three hard plosives, it has zero conflicts in software, the metaphor naturally extends to any construction ops feature, and it sounds like a tool they'd choose to use rather than software that was chosen for them.

If Gangbox feels too informal or the domain proves unacquirable, Kerf is the close second — one syllable, domain is for sale, follows the tool brand pattern (STIHL, Klein, Bosch). Bildek is the Procore-style coined option if you want maximum expansion runway and are willing to invest in building meaning from zero.

Why Gangbox wins from the worker's perspective

Foreman
"Check the gangbox"

Already says this phrase 10x daily. Zero learning curve. Feels like his tool, not an office mandate.

Project Engineer
"Log it in Gangbox"

Sounds like something that belongs on a jobsite. Opens it by default, not just for safety tasks.

Safety Director
"Our whole crew uses Gangbox"

Her tool is the company's tool. Platform, not point solution. Career-advancing position.

Company Owner
"We run everything through Gangbox"

Sounds like an operations platform. Evaluates on broad ROI, not just safety compliance.

Why not Kerf

The product has already outgrown the name. Multi-jurisdiction compliance, environmental monitoring, equipment management, project management, worker management, voice transcription, AI doc generation — this is a construction operations platform. Launching as "Kerf" means every non-safety feature fights the name for attention from day one.

There is zero brand equity to protect. No customers, no SEO, no contracts. The switching cost of choosing a different launch name is literally zero. This is the only moment in the company's life where the name is free to change.

Every customer acquired under "Kerf" makes a future rename more expensive. If the platform ambition is real, the name should match it from launch.

Next Steps
What To Do Now
  1. WHOIS check on gangbox.com — determine owner, acquisition cost. Check kerf.build and bildek.com as backups
  2. Trademark search — USPTO search for "Gangbox" in software/SaaS classes. The word is a generic construction term, which can complicate trademark registration but also means no one else can own it exclusively
  3. Say it out loud for a week — use the name in sentences. "We're building Gangbox." "Check out gangbox.com." "Gangbox just added project scheduling." Does it still feel right after 100 repetitions?
  4. Ask 5 construction workers — show them the name cold, no context. First reaction matters more than explained reaction. Do they get it? Do they respect it?
  5. If it clears all checks, rename the repo and codebase before launch — this is free right now. It will never be this cheap again.
