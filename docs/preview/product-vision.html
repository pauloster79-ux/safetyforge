1. The World We're Building Toward

It is 2029. The construction industry has changed — not in the way the trade magazines predicted, with drones and robots and 3D-printed houses, but in the way that actually matters: the contractor who used to juggle six apps, three binders, and a filing cabinet now runs everything through one tool. Safety, daily logs, time tracking, quality inspections, estimating, job costing, sub compliance, scheduling — all of it, through a single conversational interface that speaks the language of the jobsite.

The change came from a platform that started with safety compliance and expanded outward, using a knowledge graph that connects every worker, every certification, every project, every regulation, every cost line, and every daily log entry into one intelligent system. The contractor talks to Kerf — by chat or by voice — and Kerf understands, acts, and remembers.

Marco Gutierrez runs a concrete crew of 22 in Phoenix. Today, Marco opens Kerf at 5:30 AM. The morning brief is waiting. He walks the site with his phone recording. One site walk, three reports, no forms. His EMR is 0.78. He captured $34,000 in variations last year that he would have previously eaten.

Sarah Chen owns a 45-person electrical firm in Atlanta. She estimates a job by walking the site and talking to Kerf — 20 minutes during the walkthrough. Job costing happens automatically. Variations are flagged before she knows about them.

Jake Torres, a solo electrician, talks to Kerf while standing in the client's kitchen — "200-amp panel upgrade, 12 new circuits, 6 receptacles in the island" — and has a structured estimate in 30 seconds. After 20 jobs, Kerf knows what his panel upgrades actually cost.

This is the world Kerf exists to build. Not a world where construction paperwork is easier to fill out, but a world where documentation is a natural byproduct of doing the work.

Eleven hundred people died on construction sites in the United States last year. Most worked for companies with fewer than 50 employees. We are building the thing that changes that.

2. Our Beliefs

We believe safety is the foundation, not a silo. Safety touches everything — scheduling, workforce, equipment, quality, compliance, costs. A platform that starts with safety and expands outward has a natural architecture.
We believe documentation should be a byproduct of doing the work, not a separate activity. If a contractor has to stop building to start documenting, we have failed.
We believe every worker deserves information in their language, regardless of their employer's budget or their country. Multilingual support is not a premium feature — it is a fundamental obligation that scales with every market we enter.
We believe the person running the work is the most important user in construction technology, and the most ignored. Whether a solo electrician or a 100-person superintendent — we build for them first.
We believe small contractors are not less safe because they care less — they are less safe because they have less help.
We believe AI in construction must be auditable, cited, and humble. Every recommendation traces back to a specific standard or data point.
We believe compliance should be a competitive advantage, not just a cost of doing business.
We believe the phone in the contractor's pocket is the most powerful tool on the jobsite.
We believe one tool is better than six. Consolidation is not a feature — it is the product.
We believe the estimate, the task list, and the cost report are the same data. One data structure flows through its lifecycle: estimated, then scheduled, then tracked, then analysed. No re-entry. No reconciliation.
We believe financial intelligence should be derived, not entered. Kerf derives financial insight from data the contractor already captures in daily operations.
We believe the knowledge graph is the moat, not the AI. Without the graph, AI is guessing. With it, AI is traversing. That structural difference is the defensible advantage.
We believe every conversation is data. Every interaction is recorded, linked to the entities it touches, and available for the agent to draw on. The contractor's knowledge stops walking out the door.
We believe every document the contractor uploads makes the system smarter. Documents are not attachments — they are intelligence.

3. What Kerf Does

Kerf supports every process a contractor runs, from finding work through getting paid. The data structures are scale-invariant — a solo electrician and a 100-person sub run the same processes; only the complexity and concurrency scale up. Every process is accessed conversationally (chat or voice), every interaction is recorded in the knowledge graph, and the agent acts within boundaries the contractor controls.

3.1 Find and Qualify Work

A lead arrives. The contractor tells Kerf about it. The system creates a Project in "lead" status, linked to the client Contact and any documents received. The agent assesses: is this the right kind of work? Do we have the right certifications? What is our capacity? How has this GC paid us before?

Contractor does
"Peterson GC wants us to bid the electrical on a medical office on 4th Street."
Agent does
Creates the project. Links the GC contact. Checks crew availability. Surfaces past job history. Flags certification gaps for the work type.

3.2 Estimate and Price

The contractor walks the site or reviews plans and talks through what they see. The agent captures WorkItems — discrete pieces of deliverable work, each with labour and material requirements. WorkItems can be grouped into WorkPackages for larger projects, or listed flat for simple jobs.

Each WorkItem carries its own cost estimate: labour hours × rate, materials × unit cost, plus equipment. Costs are bottom-up — the project total is the sum of its work items plus margin.

When the contractor uploads plans, specs, or other project documents, the system reads them — extracting fixture counts, dimensions, material requirements, and specification clauses. The agent cross-references plan quantities against the estimate and flags requirements not yet covered.

Contractor does
Walks the site, talks through what they see. Uploads the plans. Reviews and adjusts.
Agent does
Captures structured WorkItems from conversation. Extracts quantities from uploaded plans. Applies historical labour rates and current supplier pricing. Flags regulatory requirements. Calculates margin and total.

3.3 Propose and Win

The contractor sends a proposal referencing a versioned Estimate — a snapshot of WorkItems at agreed prices. When accepted, it becomes the basis for a Contract.

The project status moves from "lead" through "estimating" to "proposed" to "awarded." A lead IS a Project — no separate entity, no data re-entry. The estimate IS the scope.

Contractor does
"Send the proposal to Peterson GC." Reviews contract terms.
Agent does
Generates the proposal document. Tracks status. Parses the signed contract for key terms (retention, payment schedule, milestones). Links everything to the project graph.

3.4 Plan and Mobilise

WorkItems from the estimate become the schedule — they already have labour, materials, and assignments. The contractor adds dates, assigns workers or crews, and sets dependencies.

Scheduling is a weekly lookahead — a rolling 2-4 week view. Connected to: worker certifications, equipment availability, weather, trade sequencing, and cost impact.

Contractor does
Assigns crews and dates. Adjusts the lookahead as conditions change.
Agent does
Generates the initial schedule from WorkItems. Detects conflicts with certifications, equipment, weather, and dependencies. Orders materials. Sends notifications.

3.5 Execute and Document

This is where the contractor spends 90% of their time. Every day on every active project:

Daily operations. Workers clock in (one tap, GPS-verified). Toolbox talk in the workforce's languages. Site walk — narrating and photographing. Daily log 80% pre-filled. One site walk, three reports, no forms.

Safety. Inspections, hazard observations, incident reporting — all voice-first. The agent classifies hazards, generates reports, assigns corrective actions, tracks to closure.

Quality. Same inspection engine, different templates. Deficiency items tracked through identification, assignment, correction, and verification.

Time tracking. GPS-verified, linked to the specific WorkItem being performed. Time entries automatically become cost data.

Progress tracking. As WorkItems are completed, the project's progress updates automatically. What work remains? What percentage is complete? On track against the estimate?

Contractor does
The work. Talks to Kerf during their normal routine.
Agent does
Captures structured data from every conversation, photo, and time entry. Auto-populates the daily log. Runs safety checks. Tracks quality deficiencies. Monitors progress against estimate and schedule. Flags issues proactively.

3.6 Manage Money

Job costing is auto-derived — never entered manually. Time entries flow to labour cost. Material deliveries flow to material cost.

Variations (scope changes — known as change orders in the US) are detected automatically. When the daily log mentions work outside the original scope, the agent assembles the evidence chain and drafts a variation. Contractors capture revenue they currently miss.

Contractor does
Asks "how are we doing on this job?"
Agent does
Traverses the graph: time entries vs estimated hours, material deliveries vs estimated materials, approved variations, delays documented in daily logs. Returns a narrative answer with reasoning. Proactively flags cost overruns and recommends actions.

3.7 Close Out

Deficiency list generated from quality inspections. Each item tracked to verified closure. As-built documentation assembled from daily logs, photos, and inspection records. Warranty obligations recorded.

Contractor does
Final walkthrough. Reviews deficiency list. Confirms completion.
Agent does
Generates deficiency list. Tracks each item. Assembles closeout documentation. Records warranty terms.

3.8 Get Paid

Invoice lines reference specific WorkItems and their completion percentage. Approved variations included. Kerf never replaces the accounting system — QuickBooks (or Xero, or Sage) handles general ledger, tax, and payroll. Kerf handles the intelligence.

Contractor does
"Bill Peterson GC for this month's progress."
Agent does
Calculates percent complete from WorkItem progress. Includes approved variations not yet billed. Generates the invoice. Syncs to accounting system. Monitors payment. Alerts if late.

3.9 Learn

Did we make money, and why? The agent traverses the full graph — estimated vs actual by WorkItem, crew productivity, delays, variations captured vs missed, material waste, schedule adherence — and produces a narrative post-mortem.

This intelligence feeds forward. The next estimate draws on real historical data from the contractor's own completed projects.

Every question is a graph traversal, not a pre-built report. "Which jobs made the most money this year, and why?" "Should I bid this hospital job?" "What is my risk exposure this week?"

Contractor does
Asks questions.
Agent does
Traverses the connected graph across every dimension. Answers in plain language with reasoning. Carries the learning forward into future estimates and decisions.

3.10 Cross-Cutting: Safety and Compliance

Safety runs through every stage. The knowledge graph encodes regulatory requirements as traversable structure. The agent checks compliance continuously — not as a separate audit, but as a natural consequence of everything being connected.

Safety data enriches everything else. Time tracking connects to fatigue analysis. Daily logs surface incident patterns. Quality deficiencies correlate with schedule pressure. The morning brief assembles risk scores, weather hazards, expiring certifications, and schedule conflicts automatically.

3.11 Cross-Cutting: Subcontractor Management

When working as a sub: compliance documentation, insurance certificates, prequalification submissions. When managing subs as a GC: real-time visibility into compliance, training currency, insurance validity, and performance metrics.

Insurance certificates AI-parsed. Renewal reminders automatic. Performance scoring from safety data, inspection results, and corrective action closure times.

3.12 Cross-Cutting: The Agent

The agent acts within boundaries the contractor controls:

  • Autonomous: Send cert reminders, check sub insurance, generate reports, flag compliance issues, prepare morning briefs, detect potential variations, monitor payments, track weather against schedule
  • Propose and confirm: Draft variations with evidence, suggest estimate adjustments, recommend schedule changes, identify cost overruns, generate invoices
  • Contractor approves: Place material orders, submit proposals, send invoices, accept contract terms, modify schedules affecting other parties

The agent's memory accumulates. After a year, it knows the contractor's labour rates, crew productivity, GC relationships, supplier preferences, and decision-making patterns. The system that has seen 50 of your jobs knows your business better than any spreadsheet ever could.

4. The Intelligence Architecture

Layer 1 — System of Truth

The Knowledge Graph (Neo4j)

All structured entities and their relationships. Decisions and insights from conversations. Regulatory rules as traversable structure. Requirements from uploaded documents. Cypher traversal. Deterministic, auditable, citable.

Layer 2 — System of Context

The Semantic Layer (Vector Index within Neo4j)

Embeddings of everything unstructured — stored as native properties on graph nodes. Neo4j 5.11+ supports HNSW vector indexes. Not a separate database. Hybrid queries combine semantic similarity with graph traversal in a single Cypher query.

Layer 3 — System of Record

The Document Store (Object Storage)

Raw files at full fidelity — PDFs, photos, voice recordings, plan sheets, contracts. Original format, permanently retained.

Conversation Memory

Every conversation produces a Conversation node linked to: projects discussed, entities referenced, decisions made (with reasoning), institutional knowledge expressed, and actions triggered. Full transcripts retained 90 days. Summaries, decisions, insights, and embeddings retained indefinitely.

1. Three taps or less for any field task.
2. If a contractor cannot finish it in two minutes, it does not ship.
3. Every language is a first-class language. EN+ES (US), EN+PL+RO (UK), EN+FR (Canada), EN+ZH+VI (Australia).
4. Every AI output must cite the specific standard.
5. We never require forms when conversation will do.
6. The system gets smarter every day, but the user never gets dumber.

9. We show the ROI in dollars, not dashboards.

9. What We Won't Build

Template library. The AI IS the template.

General-purpose inspection platform. Construction only.

Safety training LMS. Micro-training, not courses.

Enterprise accounts. We serve the 750,000 who have nothing.

Worker performance tracking. Trust is non-negotiable.

Hardware or wearables. The phone is sufficient.

Full CPM scheduling. Weekly lookahead, not Primavera.

Trade-specific estimating databases. The contractor's own historical data is more accurate.

Replace the accounting system. Kerf handles intelligence. QuickBooks handles accounting.

12. The Endgame

The fatality rate for small contractors drops below the large-contractor rate for the first time. The paper timesheet is dead. The kitchen-table estimating session is dead. The contractor talks to Kerf, and Kerf handles the rest.

The contractor who used to guess on bids now knows what their jobs actually cost. The variations that used to get eaten are captured automatically. The job that used to lose money without anyone noticing is flagged in week two.

Construction workers around the world receive safety information in their language, every day. Thousands of workers who would have been injured go home safe.

That is the endgame. Not a software company. A safer, better-run industry.
