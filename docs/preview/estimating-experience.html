Scenario 1: Jake Torres — Solo Electrician

Jake Torres

Solo electrician • Phoenix, AZ • Revenue ~$180K/year • Residential and light commercial

Licensed electrician, works alone or with one helper. Panel upgrades, kitchen remodels, small tenant fit-outs. Estimates in his head. No written proposals. Tracks nothing.

Job: Panel upgrade and circuit additions for a homeowner renovation.

What Kerf Knows About Jake (after 15 completed jobs)

  • His actual panel upgrade labour averages 6.2 hours (he used to guess 4–5)
  • His receptacle install rate is 0.38 hours/outlet in renovations (0.28 in new construction)
  • He marks up materials 15% and targets 20% margin on labour
  • He always forgets to include permit fees
  • He undercharges for attic work (his actual rate is 40% slower than standard ceiling work)
  • His helper costs him $28/hour loaded
  • He lost $1,400 on the Greenfield job because the homeowner added 4 circuits mid-job and he didn't document it as extra work
  • City Electric Supply is his regular supplier; he has current pricing from his last PO

Jake

Jake
Direct to homeowner.

Jake
Main panel is in the garage, 150-amp Federal Pacific Stab-Lok. Replacing with 200-amp, 40-space. Service entrance cable from the meter is maybe 15 feet, it's on the other side of the wall. I'll need to coordinate the utility disconnect with APS.
Kerf

Jake

Kerf
Flagged as exclusion with note. Keep going — what's happening in the kitchen?
Jake
OK so she's doing a full kitchen remodel. I need: 2 dedicated 20-amp circuits for countertop receptacles — GFCI. 1 dedicated circuit for the dishwasher. 1 dedicated circuit for the disposal. She's adding a gas range so I just need the igniter outlet behind it. Microwave circuit above. 6 receptacles along the countertop, plus 2 on the island — those'll need a floor box. And she wants under-cabinet LED lighting on a dimmer.
Kerf
That's 6 new dedicated circuits plus the general receptacle and lighting work. A couple of things based on your past jobs:
  1. The island floor box — your last two floor box installs in slab-on-grade averaged 1.8 hours, not the 1 hour you usually estimate. This house is slab-on-grade from the listing data. Want to use 1.8?
  2. Under-cabinet LEDs — are you supplying the fixtures, or is the homeowner buying them and you're just doing the wiring?
  3. 2021 NEC requires AFCI protection on all kitchen circuits in Arizona. That adds ~$45 per breaker over standard. Are you pricing AFCI breakers?
Jake
Good catches. Yeah use the 1.8 for the floor box. She's buying the LED fixtures, I'm just running the low-voltage wire and the transformer. And yes on the AFCI breakers.
Kerf
Got it. Anything else in the kitchen or other rooms?
Jake
That's it. Just the panel and the kitchen.
Kerf
OK, pulling this together. One more thing — your last 4 residential jobs, you didn't include the permit fee in your price and then ate it. Phoenix residential electrical permit for this scope is $134. Want to include it as a line item or exclude it in the terms?
Jake
Include it. You're right, I keep eating that.
Kerf
Here's your estimate. I've used your actual labour rates from your last 15 jobs, current City Electric Supply pricing from your March PO, and the AFCI requirement. Take a look.

Scenario 2: Sarah Chen — 45-Person Electrical Contractor

Sarah Chen

Chen Electrical Services • Atlanta, GA • 45 employees, 3 crews • Revenue ~$8M/year

Commercial and light industrial electrical contractor. Sarah is owner, lead estimator, and PM on major jobs. Subs to GCs on most work. Has a part-time office admin.

Job: Electrical fit-out of a 2-storey medical office building, 8,400 SF. General contractor is Peterson GC. Competitive bid — Peterson is getting 3–4 electrical quotes.

What Kerf Knows About Sarah (after 38 completed jobs)

  • Her crew's actual productivity on commercial receptacle install is 0.35 man-hours/device (industry book rate is 0.25, but her crews work in renovation conditions 60% of the time)
  • Her loaded labour rate (journeyman) is $68.40/hour including burden (base $44, 36% on-cost, 15% non-productive time)
  • Her apprentice loaded rate is $38.20/hour
  • She typically runs a 2-person crew (1 journeyman + 1 apprentice) on medical office work
  • Her actual conduit productivity is 32 LF/hour for EMT in commercial drop ceilings (book rate is 40)
  • She targets 18–22% margin on GC work, higher on direct-to-owner
  • Peterson GC has delayed site access on 3 of the last 5 jobs, averaging 11 days late
  • Peterson's standard subcontract has a 10% retention clause and 45-day payment terms
  • Her material markup is 10% on GC jobs
  • She has current pricing from City Electric Supply (primary) and Graybar (secondary)
  • She lost $12K on the Buckhead Dental job because she priced prelims as a lump sum and Peterson delayed her 6 weeks — she couldn't substantiate a delay claim because her overhead wasn't broken out by duration
  • Medical office work requires dedicated circuits for every exam room — she's done 4 medical offices and knows the typical scope

The Conversation

Sarah
(at her desk after the site walk, reviewing the plans Peterson sent)
Starting an estimate. Peterson GC, 4th Street medical office. They sent the bid package yesterday. I walked it this morning and took photos. Let me upload the plans and specs.
Kerf
Project created — Peterson GC, 4th Street Medical Office. I see you've uploaded 14 files. Let me process those. While I'm reading, what are the key bid conditions?
Sarah
Lump sum subcontract. They want our price by Friday the 18th. Their schedule shows electrical rough-in starting June 2nd, substantial completion October 15th — about 19 weeks. They're getting three other electrical quotes.
Kerf
Got it. A few things before we start on the scope:
  1. Peterson GC access history. On your last 5 Peterson jobs, they've been late on site access 3 times, averaging 11 days. Your Buckhead Dental loss was partly because your prelims weren't duration-based and you couldn't claim the delay. Do you want to structure your prelims by duration this time so we have a variation basis if they're late again?
  2. Peterson's subcontract terms. Their standard form has 10% retention and net-45 payment. On your last job, they held retention for 97 days past substantial completion. Do you want to flag retention release timing in your offer?
  3. Medical office scope. Based on your last 4 medical offices, there are typically dedicated circuit requirements for each exam room that aren't always shown on the electrical drawings — imaging equipment, autoclaves, suction units. The mechanical drawings usually show the equipment but the electrical drawings sometimes miss the dedicated circuits. Want me to cross-reference when I finish reading the plans?
Sarah
Yes to all three. Good catch on the cross-reference — that's exactly what happened on Peachtree Medical.
Kerf
I've finished reading the plans and specifications. Here's my initial scope summary:

Building: 2-storey medical office, 8,400 SF (4,200 per floor). Steel frame, drop ceiling, concrete slab on grade.

Electrical scope from drawings:
  • 200A 3-phase service, 2 distribution panels (one per floor)
  • 84 standard receptacles
  • 22 dedicated receptacles (exam rooms, lab, imaging, break room appliances)
  • 16 data/low-voltage locations (cabling by others, we provide conduit and boxes)
  • 143 light fixtures (mix of 2×4 troffers, exam lights, can lights in corridors)
  • 12 switches and 6 dimmers
  • 8 emergency/exit lights with battery backup
  • Fire alarm conduit and boxes (FA system by fire alarm contractor, we provide raceways)
  • Mechanical equipment connections: 2 RTUs, 1 split system, 1 exhaust fan
Spec requirements noted:
  • All wiring in EMT conduit (no MC cable)
  • Arc-fault protection on all 15A and 20A circuits per 2020 NEC
  • Generator transfer switch and connection (generator by owner, we install ATS and connection)
  • Metering for each tenant space (3 meters)
Cross-reference flag: The mechanical drawings show an autoclave in the sterilisation room and a panoramic X-ray unit in imaging. Neither has a corresponding dedicated circuit on the electrical drawings. Based on your Peachtree Medical job, the autoclave needed a 30A 208V circuit and the X-ray unit needed a 20A dedicated with isolated ground. Want me to include these and note them as plan discrepancies in your offer?
Sarah
Yes. Include them in our price but call them out as items we added based on the mechanical drawings. If they're in, we've covered them. If they strip them, we can reduce. Either way, we look thorough and it protects us from a variation dispute later.
Kerf
Smart. I also notice the spec calls for EMT throughout — no MC cable. Your MC cable productivity is about 20% faster than EMT in drop ceilings. Going to EMT adds roughly 180 labour hours to this job compared to MC. Do you want to note in your offer that a value engineering option exists to switch to MC cable for corridors and non-patient areas, with a potential saving? It might differentiate your bid.
Sarah
Yes. Show the base price with EMT as specified, and a VE option for MC in non-clinical areas. What's the saving look like?
Kerf
Based on your crew rates and the conduit quantities, switching corridors and admin areas to MC cable would save approximately $8,200 in labour and add approximately $2,100 in material cost (MC cable costs more per foot than EMT). Net saving to Peterson: roughly $6,100. I'll include it as an alternate.
Sarah
Perfect. Let's build this out. Give me the full estimate.

The agent builds the complete estimate from the plan takeoff, Sarah's historical productivity rates, current supplier pricing, and the scope decisions made in conversation. Sarah reviews the detailed worksheets, adjusts a few items based on site conditions she noted during the walk, and approves the final numbers. That review process takes about 20 minutes.

What the Agent Did That Protects Sarah's Margin

1
Duration-based prelims (the Buckhead Dental fix). Section 8.0 breaks supervision and PM into weekly rates × 19 weeks. Section 4.2 explicitly states that if the project extends beyond 19 weeks, the weekly overhead rates apply. If Peterson delays site access by 3 weeks (which they've done on 3 of the last 5 jobs), Sarah's variation claim is arithmetic: 3 weeks × ($672 + $547) = $3,657. No argument. The rates are in the offer.
2
Mobilisation count qualification. Section 4.2 explicitly states one mob/demob is included, with additional mobs at $2,400 each. If Peterson's schedule changes force Sarah to demobilise and return, she has a contractual basis to charge.
3
The autoclave/X-ray cross-reference. Items 7.1 and 7.2 demonstrate that Sarah read the full drawing set, not just the electrical pages. This protects her from missing scope and makes her bid look more thorough than competitors who only read the E-sheets. If Peterson strips them, she offers a credit — she looks collaborative.
4
The VE option. The MC cable alternate saves Peterson $6,100 and differentiates Sarah's bid. If Peterson is comparing 4 bids and Sarah's is $3K higher than the lowest, but she's offering a $6,100 VE option, she may win on net price while having a more comprehensive base scope.
5
Variation rates in the offer. Section 5 establishes the rates for additional work before the contract is signed. Note the rates include margin — journeyman at $95/hr vs. loaded cost of $68.40/hr = 39% markup covering overhead and profit on variations.
6
Material escalation protection. Section 6 locks material pricing for 90 days, not indefinitely. If Peterson takes 4 months to execute the subcontract, Sarah isn't locked into April pricing on copper she buys in August.
7
Retention and payment terms. Section 6 requests 5% retention (not Peterson's standard 10%) and release at completion of her scope, not the overall project. Peterson held retention for 97 days past substantial completion on the last job.
8
Access and stacking clauses. Section 4.4 qualifies that productivity loss from excessive trade stacking or out-of-sequence work is claimable. This is the structural protection for things outside Sarah's control.

The Learning Loop

Both proposals get better with every job. The graph captures:

From Jake's jobs:
  • Actual hours vs. estimated hours per task type (panel swap, receptacle install, floor box, etc.)
  • Which exclusions he ended up needing (the asbestos on the Verde Valley job becomes a permanent checklist item)
  • Change orders captured vs. missed (after Greenfield, the agent always includes an additional work clause)
  • Material cost trends from his POs
From Sarah's jobs:
  • Crew productivity by work type, building type, and GC (Peterson jobs run 8% slower due to coordination issues)
  • Variation success rate by qualification type (duration-based prelim claims have 90% approval; productivity impact claims have 40%)
  • Which GCs pay on time and which don't (affects cash flow assumptions and the retention negotiation)
  • Which scope items are consistently missed on medical office drawings
  • VE option acceptance rate (Peterson has accepted 3 of 5 VE proposals)
The compound effect: After 50 jobs, the agent doesn't just calculate faster — it knows Sarah's business better than she does. It knows which qualifications actually protect her, which GCs are worth the risk, and where her crews consistently over- or under-perform against her estimates. That institutional knowledge currently lives in Sarah's head and disappears if she gets sick, retires, or loses the spreadsheet.

What This Means for Kerf's Data Model

The estimating experience requires entities and relationships that flow through the full lifecycle:

Conversation (voice/chat) → extracts WorkItems with labour, material, equipment quantities → grouped into WorkPackages (divisions) → with Assumptions (structured, referenceable) → with Exclusions (structured, referenceable) → with AlternateOptions (VE proposals, linked to affected WorkItems) → snapshot as Estimate (versioned, dated) → generates Proposal document (PDF) → on award: WorkItems become ScheduleTasks → on execution: TimeEntries charged to WorkItems → on completion: Actual vs. Estimated feeds back to productivity rates The terms and conditions are not free text bolted onto a spreadsheet. They are structured data in the graph — queryable, learnable, referenceable in future estimates and in variation claims during construction.
