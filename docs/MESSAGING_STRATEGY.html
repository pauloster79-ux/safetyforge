Messaging Strategy

What to say, how to say it, and why it will cut through — April 2026

Diagnosis: Why the Current Messaging Won’t Scale

The current landing page is well-written and effective for H1 (safety-only). But it has three structural problems as Kerf expands:

1. It sells safety software, not a construction operating system. Every section is OSHA-centric — penalties, citations, compliance gaps. This attracts the contractor who Googles “OSHA fall protection plan” at 11 PM. It does not attract the contractor who is drowning in six apps, paper timesheets, and Sunday-night catch-up sessions. As H2 features ship, the site must speak to the broader pain.
2. It leads with fear, not time. The hero (“Know exactly where you stand before OSHA walks on site”) is a fear message. Research shows time savings is the #1 universal motivator for small contractors. Fear creates anxiety; time savings creates desire. Jobber leads with “12 hours+ saved per week.” ServiceTitan leads with “25% revenue increase.” Fear should be a supporting lever, not the headline.
3. It doesn’t tell Marco’s story. The vision document has the most compelling narrative in the entire codebase: one site walk, talk into your phone, three reports write themselves, no forms. The landing page never mentions voice input, daily logs, time tracking, or the “documentation as a byproduct of work” thesis. The product principle “the best documentation is the documentation that writes itself” should be the messaging foundation, not just an internal belief.

What’s Working (Keep These)

  • Pain-point scenarios (“If any of this is you”) — specific, emotionally resonant, mirror real contractor language
  • Testimonials with specifics — Mike R. saves $1,200/plan, Sarah K. avoids $16K fine
  • Low-friction CTAs — “Try it free, no credit card” and “See what OSHA would find”
  • Regulatory citations throughout — builds trust with OSHA-literate buyers
  • Price anchoring — comparing $99/mo to $1,500/SSSP consultant fee

The Core Narrative: “One Walk. Everything Done.”

The vision document contains the most powerful marketing story Kerf has. It is currently buried in an internal doc. It should be the centrepiece of the marketing site.

Before Kerf — Marco’s Morning
  1. Check weather on phone
  2. Open App 1 for safety checklist
  3. Open App 2 for daily log
  4. Open App 3 for time tracking
  5. Open email for RFI responses
  6. Check spreadsheet for schedule
  7. Sunday night: reconcile all of it

6 apps. Paper timesheets. Fiction written from memory.

With Kerf — Marco’s Morning
  1. Open Kerf. Morning brief is waiting.
  2. Deliver toolbox talk. Crew signs on their phones.
  3. Walk the site for 8 minutes, talking and taking photos.
  4. Kerf writes the safety inspection, quality report, and daily log.
  5. Review. Two taps. Done.

One tool. One walk. Three reports. No forms.

This is the cut-through message. No competitor can tell this story. SafetyCulture does inspections. Raken does daily logs. ClockShark does time tracking. Nobody does all three from one site walk using voice and camera. The “one walk, everything done” narrative is unique to Kerf and impossible to claim without the technology to back it up.

Summary: Three Things That Will Cut Through
1. “One walk, everything done.” No competitor can say this. It is specific, demonstrable, and immediately understood by anyone who has ever done a site walk with a clipboard. It leads with time (primary lever), implies technology depth (voice + AI), and differentiates from every template-based, form-filling competitor. This is the headline.
2. “Replace your safety binder, daily log app, time tracker, and paper forms.” This names the tools being replaced. Specificity beats abstraction. “All-in-one platform” is generic. “Replace your safety binder, daily log app, time tracker, and paper forms” is a checklist the contractor can verify against their own desk. The consolidation narrative is the value justification — $299 vs $1,275 is unanswerable.
3. “In English and Spanish. Because a third of your crew shouldn’t get worse safety information because of the language they speak.” This is a values statement disguised as a feature. It signals that Kerf understands who actually works on construction sites. No competitor leads with bilingual as a moral position rather than a checkbox feature. It differentiates emotionally, builds trust with Latino-owned firms, and is memorable precisely because nobody else says it.
